Obstruction Circuits

An obstruction circuit is a law-relative minimal bad molecule. It turns architectural failure from a vague smell into the smallest selected atom configuration that still violates a design law.

Research question

When a design principle fails, what is the smallest part of the architecture responsible for the failure?

AAT answers with obstruction circuits. A law failure is not merely a warning label. It becomes a finite molecule whose atom surface is bad for a selected law, and whose proper submolecules are no longer bad. That minimality is what makes the diagnostic useful.

Badness

Badness is always law-relative. A molecule is not bad in the abstract; it is bad for a selected DesignLaw. The same molecule may violate a substitution law, satisfy a layering law, and remain unevaluated by a runtime law.

Definition 1.1. A bad molecule is a molecule selected by a law's Bad predicate. It is not necessarily a global architecture failure; it is a failure under the selected law and selected molecule boundary.

Proposition 1.2. Badness does not create atoms. It evaluates an already-selected atom molecule.

Proof. DesignLaw is defined over molecules from an atom axiom system, and DesignLaw.does_not_create_atoms returns the root non-generation boundary. The law marks selected configurations as bad; it does not change which atoms exist.

Minimality

Minimality separates an obstruction circuit from an arbitrary failing region. A large subsystem can be bad because it contains a smaller bad molecule. The obstruction circuit is the minimal molecule that still carries the failure.

Definition 2.1. An ObstructionCircuit is a MinimalBadMolecule: a law-relative bad molecule with no proper bad submolecule.

Proposition 2.2. Every obstruction circuit is bad for its selected law, and every proper submolecule is not bad for that law.

Proof. Lean defines ObstructionCircuit law M as MinimalBadMolecule law M. The theorem obstructionCircuit_bad projects the badness component. The theorem obstructionCircuit_minimal projects the minimality component and proves that any proper submolecule is not bad.

Finite molecule universe

A finite molecule universe records the selected search space in which obstruction circuits are found. It does not say that all possible molecules in the world were searched. It says that, inside the selected universe, every selected bad molecule has a selected minimal bad submolecule.

Definition 3.1. A FiniteMoleculeUniverse is a selected finite witness universe for bad-molecule search. Its coverage and exactness boundaries are part of the diagnostic claim.

Proposition 3.2. In a finite molecule universe, a selected bad molecule contains a selected obstruction circuit below it.

Proof. The theorem FiniteMoleculeUniverse.contains_minimal_bad applies the universe's minimalOf field to a selected bad molecule M. It returns a molecule N that is selected, is an obstruction circuit, and is a submolecule of M.

Proposition 3.3. If badness is upward-closed in the selected universe, then a selected molecule is bad exactly when it contains a selected obstruction circuit.

Proof. Lean packages upward closure as BadUpwardClosed law. The theorem FiniteMoleculeUniverse.bad_iff_contains_obstruction_circuit proves both directions: badness yields a minimal bad submolecule by minimalOf, and a contained obstruction circuit yields badness by upward closure.

Lawfulness bridge

Obstruction absence is not automatically the same as lawfulness. AAT connects them with a bridge: every selected bad molecule must produce a selected obstruction circuit, and every selected obstruction circuit must be bad for the law.

Proposition 4.1. Lawfulness over a selected molecule boundary is equivalent to no required obstruction circuit only when the LawfulnessBridge assumptions are supplied.

Proof. LawfulnessBridge.lawful_iff_no_obstructionCircuit proves the equivalence. The forward direction uses circuitBad: a required circuit is bad, contradicting lawfulness. The backward direction uses badWitnessComplete: any bad required molecule yields a required circuit, contradicting no-circuit.

Review reading

Obstruction circuits change the shape of design review. A reviewer does not merely say "this smells like a cycle" or "this violates substitution." The AAT reading asks for the law, the molecule, the minimality claim, and the selected universe that makes the witness complete enough to trust.

Law
Which design law is being evaluated?
Bad molecule
Which finite atom configuration violates the law?
Minimality
Which atoms can be removed before the violation disappears?
Coverage
Which molecule universe was searched for witnesses?
Exactness
Why does this witness mean the selected law failed?

Proposition 5.1. A design review finding becomes an AAT obstruction circuit only when it carries law-relative badness, minimality, and selected universe boundaries.

Non-conclusion 5.2. A tool warning, linter message, metric spike, or reviewer intuition is not automatically an obstruction circuit. It can become evidence for one after the law, molecule, minimality, coverage, and exactness are supplied.

Counterexamples and non-conclusions

Counterexample 7.1. A large subsystem can be bad without being an obstruction circuit. If a smaller proper submolecule is already bad, the large subsystem is not minimal.

Counterexample 7.2. A warning about a dependency edge is not a cycle obstruction unless the selected molecule contains a law-relative bad cycle and the proper submolecules fail to be bad.

Counterexample 7.3. No obstruction circuit found in a selected finite universe does not imply global lawfulness. The witness may lie outside the selected molecule universe or outside the observation coverage.

Non-conclusion 7.4. Obstruction circuits do not prove runtime incidents, business harm, user-visible failure, or SFT forecast correctness by themselves. They prove a selected law-relative minimal bad molecule.

Formal reading

The Lean surface for this chapter is in Formal/Arch/AAT/Circuit.lean. It defines obstruction circuits as minimal bad molecules, adds finite molecule universes for selected witness search, and proves the lawfulness / no-obstruction bridge when the bridge assumptions are provided.

Formal status 8.1. MinimalBadMolecule, ObstructionCircuit, FiniteMoleculeUniverse, LawfulWithinMoleculeConfiguration, NoRequiredObstructionCircuit, and LawfulnessBridge are defined in Lean.

Formal status 8.2. The accessor theorems for circuit badness and minimality, the finite-universe minimal-bad witness theorem, the upward-closed badness equivalence, and the lawfulness/no-circuit bridge are proved.

Chapter summary

Obstruction circuits are the diagnostic core of AAT. They identify the smallest selected molecule that still violates a selected design law. This lets architectural review name not only that something is wrong, but which atom configuration is responsible for the failure.

The chapter also fixes the boundary. Minimality, selected universe coverage, and exactness are part of the claim. Without them, a warning may be useful evidence, but it is not yet an AAT obstruction circuit.

Terms

Bad molecule
A molecule marked bad by a selected design law.
MinimalBadMolecule
A bad molecule whose proper submolecules are not bad for the selected law.
ObstructionCircuit
A law-relative minimal bad molecule.
FiniteMoleculeUniverse
The selected witness universe used to find minimal bad molecules.
BadUpwardClosed
The condition that badness propagates from a submolecule to a larger molecule.
LawfulnessBridge
The assumptions connecting selected lawfulness to selected obstruction-circuit absence.
