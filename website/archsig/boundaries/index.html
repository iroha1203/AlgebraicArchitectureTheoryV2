Evidence boundary

An evidence boundary is the explicit scope of a signal. It includes the selected source universe, policy inputs, runtime evidence, semantic observations, unavailable evidence, unsupported constructs, private data, and assumptions required for interpretation.

Measurement status

  • MeasuredThe axis was evaluated in the selected universe.
  • UnmeasuredThe artifact has no measurement for that axis.
  • UnsupportedThe adapter or command does not observe that construct.
  • Private or unavailableThe evidence may exist, but this artifact cannot inspect it.
  • Out of scopeThe axis is intentionally outside the selected run.

Claim boundary

A report can make a review claim, a policy claim, a theorem-precondition claim, or an empirical calibration claim. These levels must not be collapsed. Review evidence can guide inspection without proving an architecture theorem.

Formal promotion

Formal promotion requires bridge assumptions: extractor completeness for the selected universe, exactness of policy mapping, theorem preconditions, and proof-relevant definitions. ArchSig can report that those assumptions are satisfied, missing, or outside the artifact.

Runtime and semantic evidence

Runtime evidence and semantic evidence are powerful because architecture risk often lives outside static imports. They must remain bounded. A trace is not complete runtime semantics. An LLM observation is not a judge. A human-confirmed AIR record is stronger than a raw generated comment.

Non-conclusions

  • No scalar quality scoreArchitecture Signature is a multi-axis diagnostic.
  • No extractor completeness by defaultUnsupported dynamic behavior remains visible.
  • No causal theorem from correlationOperational records support calibration and hypotheses.
  • No automatic theorem proofMeasured review witnesses are not Lean theorem proofs.
  • No point forecastSFT artifacts describe bounded forecast structure, not exact future outcomes.
