Research question

How can architecture be computed without reducing architecture to a single graph, matrix, score, or runtime trace?

A representation is a disciplined reading surface. It exposes selected structure to computation: dependencies become graph edges, reachability becomes a category, walks become countable evidence, finite graphs become matrices, signatures become analytic values, and effects become diagrams over histories and boundaries. AAT keeps these surfaces separate from the object itself. A representation may preserve zero, reflect zero under coverage, preserve obstruction, or reflect obstruction under completeness assumptions. These are different strengths.

Graph representation

The first computable representation of an architecture object is a dependency graph. Component atoms become vertices. Dependency relation atoms become directed edges. Other atoms do not disappear; they become labels, annotations, diagrams, state transition expressions, or effect-boundary expressions.

Definition 1.1. A graph representation is the selected dependency projection of an architecture object, enriched by labels or side surfaces for atoms that are not dependency edges.

Proposition 1.2. Graph representation is not architecture equality. Two architecture objects can have the same dependency graph and differ in contract, state, semantic, effect, evidence, or law interpretation atoms.

Non-conclusion 1.3. Dependency acyclicity is a static structural reading. It does not imply semantic correctness, effect safety, replay lawfulness, runtime availability, or future repair success.

Category, walk, and path

Reachability turns the dependency graph into a thin category: there is at most one morphism from one component to another, because the morphism records reachability as a proposition. This is useful, but it forgets path count and walk length. AAT keeps walks and paths as separate computational surfaces when those quantities matter.

Surface What it remembers What it forgets
Reachable Whether one component can reach another. How many walks, which route, and how long.
ComponentCategory Identity and composition of reachability. Multiplicity of arrows, path weights, and walk history.
Walk Concrete edge-by-edge route and length. Semantic equality between alternative routes unless added separately.
Path Selected simple or representative route. All unselected walk variants.

Definition 2.1. ComponentCategory is the category whose objects are components and whose morphisms are reachability propositions.

Proposition 2.2. ComponentCategory is thin: any two morphisms between the same objects are equal.

Proof. Lean represents homs as propositions. Proof irrelevance gives equality of any two reachability witnesses.

Proposition 2.3. Walk evidence implies reachability, while reachability can be represented by a selected simple walk.

Proof idea. Reachable.of_walk maps a concrete walk to a reachability witness. Reachable.exists_simpleWalk recovers a simple-walk witness from reachability, choosing a non-repeating representative when a repeated vertex appears.

Matrix representation

A finite component universe turns a dependency graph into an adjacency matrix. Matrix powers count selected walks. Nilpotence and spectral radius then become analytic readings of the same finite static structure.

Definition 3.1. AdjacencyNilpotent is eventual zero of all powers of the finite adjacency matrix over the selected component universe.

Theorem 3.2. For a finite selected dependency graph, adjacency nilpotence, walk acyclicity, and graph acyclicity are equivalent.

Proof idea. adjacencyNilpotent_iff_walkAcyclic and adjacencyNilpotent_iff_acyclic use the finite walk counting interpretation of matrix powers. A closed walk can be repeated past any nilpotence cutoff; an acyclic finite graph has a bound after which no walk remains.

Theorem 3.3. Acyclicity forces the finite complex adjacency matrix to have spectral radius zero, while a closed walk forces positive spectral radius.

Proof. spectralRadiusOfAdjacency_eq_zero_of_acyclic reads acyclicity through a nilpotent finite complex adjacency matrix. spectralRadiusOfAdjacency_pos_of_hasClosedWalk uses repeated closed walks to refute nilpotence.

Non-conclusion 3.4. Decomposable remains the strict-layering definition. Acyclicity, finite propagation, nilpotence, and spectral conditions are connected representation readings, not extra clauses silently bundled into Decomposable.

Analytic representation

An analytic representation maps architecture states into an analytic domain: a signature, curvature value, matrix diagnostic, obstruction valuation, report axis, or other computable object. Its strength is not determined by the map alone. It is determined by the preservation and reflection theorems supplied with it.

Definition 4.1. An AnalyticRepresentation consists of a representation map, structural and analytic zero predicates, structural and analytic obstruction predicates, coverage assumptions, witness-completeness assumptions, semantic-contract coverage assumptions, and non-conclusions.

Theorem 4.2. Zero preservation and obstruction preservation are forward readings; zero reflection and obstruction reflection require the recorded coverage, witness completeness, and semantic-contract coverage assumptions.

Proof. Lean exposes the four accessors analyticZero_of_structuralZero, structuralZero_of_analyticZero, analyticObstruction_of_structuralObstruction, and structuralObstruction_of_analyticObstruction. The reflecting accessors require the additional premises explicitly.

Example 4.3. architectureSignatureAnalyticRepresentation maps an ArchitectureLawModel to its Signature v1 output. Its analytic zero is exactly selected required signature axes being available and zero.

Non-conclusion 4.4. An absent optional axis value is not an available zero certificate. none records unmeasured status; it cannot discharge a zero-reflecting theorem package.

Valuation and aggregates

Numerical readings are useful when they remain witness-relative. An obstruction valuation assigns a natural value to a selected state and selected witness. Zero may rule out that witness only when the valuation package says zero reflects absence. Aggregates are even more delicate: a scalar can help rank hotspots, but it is not a replacement for the signature vector.

Definition 5.1. An ObstructionValuation is a selected numerical reading of selected obstruction witnesses, not a complete architecture quality metric.

Proposition 5.2. Zero valuation of an individual selected witness rules out that witness obstruction; a zero-reflecting aggregate rules out all selected witnesses only through its explicit reflection premise.

Proof. no_obstruction_of_value_zero applies the witness-level zero-reflection field. noSelectedObstruction_of_zeroReflectingSum applies a caller-supplied aggregate reflection law.

Proposition 5.3. A measured nonzero analytic axis supports a selected analytic obstruction reading; an unmeasured axis blocks zero-reflecting claims.

Proof idea. supportsSelectedAnalyticObstruction_of_measuredNonzero reads some (n + 1) as a selected positive witness. not_canDischargeZeroReflectingClaim_of_unmeasured prevents none from being used as available-zero evidence.

State-transition algebra

State atoms and event atoms generate a state-transition surface. A transition law is a required diagram over history, replay, roundtrip, or compensation expressions. Lawfulness means every selected diagram commutes under the selected semantics.

Definition 6.1. StateTransitionExpr is the expression language for selected state, history, replay, roundtrip, and compensation laws.

Theorem 6.2. For finite selected state-transition cases, replay, roundtrip, compensation, and their aggregate law family are lawful exactly when the corresponding obstruction witnesses are absent.

Proof. stateReplayLawful_iff_noStateReplayObstruction, stateRoundtripLawful_iff_noStateRoundtripObstruction, stateCompensationLawful_iff_noStateCompensationObstruction, and stateTransitionLawFamilyLawful_iff_noStateTransitionLawFamilyObstruction are all instances of the generic diagram-lawfulness equivalence.

Example 6.3. Event sourcing, snapshot consistency, retry safety, migration naturality, and saga compensation can all be read as state-transition diagrams. They are not the same law; each selects its own expressions, cases, and observation semantics.

Effect law surface

Effect atoms represent actions that cross a state, service, or external boundary. Dependency graphs often miss these obstructions. A module can be statically acyclic and still send an effect too early, replay an effect unsafely, lose information across a boundary, or fail to compensate an external action.

Definition 7.1. EffectBoundaryExpr is the expression language for selected effect, boundary crossing, effect trace, replay, roundtrip, and compensation diagrams.

Theorem 7.2. For finite selected effect-boundary cases, effect replay, roundtrip, compensation, and their aggregate law family are lawful exactly when the corresponding obstruction witnesses are absent.

Proof. effectReplayLawful_iff_noEffectReplayObstruction, effectRoundtripLawful_iff_noEffectRoundtripObstruction, effectCompensationLawful_iff_noEffectCompensationObstruction, and effectBoundaryLawFamilyLawful_iff_noEffectBoundaryLawFamilyObstruction use the same finite required-diagram equivalence as the state-transition surface.

Non-conclusion 7.3. A statically clean dependency graph does not prove effect lawfulness. Effect laws require their own selected semantics and required diagrams.

Worked example

Consider a user-registration feature with a user service, a user repository, and a mail service.

The dependency graph can be acyclic, the adjacency matrix can be nilpotent, and the spectral radius can be zero while an effect-ordering obstruction remains. Sending the welcome email before the user email is durably persisted is not a dependency cycle. It is a state/effect diagram failure.

AAT therefore reads the system through multiple surfaces. The graph detects static dependency structure. The matrix detects finite walk and nilpotence structure. The signature records selected axes. The state-transition algebra checks replay, roundtrip, and compensation laws. The effect-boundary surface checks boundary effects. No single scalar replaces this product of readings.

Counterexamples and non-conclusions

Counterexample 9.1. Equal dependency graphs do not imply equal architecture objects. Contract, state, semantic, evidence, and effect atoms may differ.

Counterexample 9.2. Reachability forgets walk count and walk length. A thin category is the wrong surface for path multiplicity or weighted propagation analysis.

Counterexample 9.3. A zero aggregate is not a proof of flatness unless the selected aggregate is zero-reflecting for the selected witness universe.

Counterexample 9.4. An optional axis value of none is not a measured zero. It is unmeasured and cannot discharge a covered zero claim.

Counterexample 9.5. Static acyclicity and matrix nilpotence do not imply effect replay safety, roundtrip correctness, or compensation lawfulness.

Chapter summary

Representations make AAT executable. Graphs expose static dependency structure, categories expose reachability, walks expose routes, matrices expose finite walk and spectral readings, signatures expose selected axes, valuations expose selected numerical witnesses, and effect diagrams expose dynamic boundary laws.

The representation boundary is the point. A graph is not the whole architecture, a matrix is not a semantic proof, an aggregate is not a signature, and an unmeasured axis is not zero evidence. Computation is trustworthy only when its preservation and reflection assumptions are visible.

Terms

Graph representation
The selected dependency projection of an architecture object, with non-dependency atoms kept on other surfaces.
Thin component category
The reachability category that forgets path count and walk length.
Adjacency nilpotence
Eventual zero of finite adjacency matrix powers over a selected component universe.
AnalyticRepresentation
A map from architecture states to an analytic domain with explicit zero, obstruction, coverage, completeness, and non-conclusion fields.
ObstructionValuation
A selected numerical reading of selected obstruction witnesses.
Effect law surface
A required-diagram surface for replay, roundtrip, and compensation laws across selected effect boundaries.
