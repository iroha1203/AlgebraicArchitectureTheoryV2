Appendix

Mathematical Ambient, Claim Status, and a Finite Worked Example

The appendix collects the standardization layer of the monograph: the ambient conventions the main text works in, the precise way AAT geometry embeds into standard algebraic geometry, the ledger that fixes the claim status of every major predicate and theorem label, and finite worked examples small enough to verify by hand — a pseudo-circle Čech class, a Stanley-Reisner obstruction ideal, a monomial Tor conflict, a period pairing, an atom-family-to-geometry toy reading, and the circle-nerve SAGA instance. The main sequence of the theory starts from the Atom; this appendix is consulted when needed. Its guiding statement: AAT geometry does not replace algebraic geometry — AAT geometry is ordinary algebraic geometry plus typed architectural decoration.

How to read the claims

AAT states its claims relative to a fixed atom vocabulary, law universe, coverage topology, coefficient sheaf, and selected representation family. Three claim grades are kept distinct throughout the monograph.

Formal theorem
A mathematical proposition proved from the axioms, definitions, and stated assumptions.
Certified bounded inference
A relative inference that holds under explicitly stated coverage, exactness, or witness-completeness assumptions. It is never an unconditional absolute claim.
Analytic reading
A way of reading a constructed geometric object through graphs, signatures, periods, or metrics.

This appendix carries two audit instruments for that discipline: the claim status ledger of Section B.1 assigns a status to every major predicate and theorem label of the main text, and the label discipline of Section B.2 fixes how those labels are read. The ledger is an audit aid; the canonical statements live in the main parts, and the ledger points back to them rather than replacing them. Lean status badges on this page appear only where the AG Lean index has a corresponding ledger row; a statement without a badge is a mathematical statement in the canonical text and is not read as a current Lean proved claim.

A.1 Size and coefficient conventions

Where needed, sites are treated as small or essentially small. When size issues arise, objects and morphisms are read inside a fixed Grothendieck universe.

The coefficient ring is fixed as

k in CommRing

and from Part III onward the coordinate algebras are treated as commutative k-algebras: 

CommAlg_k

This convention is not a claim that architecture operations or runtime effects commute. It means that the coordinate geometry used to treat lawful loci as closed subschemes or derived zero loci is placed over commutative algebra.

A.2 Relative parameters

Algebro-geometric AAT is relativized to at least the following choices: 

V : AtomVocabulary
U : LawUniverse
J : CoverageTopology
k : coefficient ring

This tuple is sometimes written symbolically as \( p = (V, U, J, k) \). Even when the same architecture object is being read, changing \( V \), \( U \), \( J \), or \( k \) in general yields a different geometry. The superscripted form

\[ X^{V,U,J,k} \]

is the structure that makes explicit which vocabulary, laws, coverage, and coefficients a statement speaks within. When the main text handles finer readings, the parameter p may also include: 

selected witness family
selected signature axes
selected representation family
selected profile / repair order

This extended parameter is called a Reading, written p : Reading. Every theorem is relativized to its explicit p. About vocabularies, witnesses, axes, and profiles outside p, the theory claims neither zero, nor lawful, nor complete.

A.2.1 Parameter functoriality

Relative parameters carry comparison morphisms when the conditions line up. None of the following is automatic; each holds only when the witness ideals, restrictions, coverage, and coefficient base change are compatible.

Enlarging the law universe. When the law universe grows, with 

U subset U'
I_U subset I_{U'}

the lawful loci form a tower of closed immersions:

\[ \mathrm{Flat}_{U'}(X) \subset \mathrm{Flat}_U(X). \]

Whether earlier conclusions survive an enlarged vocabulary or law universe is therefore read as a pullback and restriction problem along this closed immersion.

Refining the coverage. When a refinement J' refines J preserves the chosen witnesses and the coefficient sheaf, the Čech complexes and sheaf cohomology acquire a comparison map

\[ H^n_J(X, \mathrm{Ob}_U) \longrightarrow H^n_{J'}(X, \mathrm{Ob}_U), \]

through which one can ask whether an obstruction class survives cover refinement.

Base change of coefficients. For a base change \( k \to k' \) satisfying a flatness condition, the coordinate algebras, obstruction ideals, Tor conflicts, and cohomology classes transport to \( k' \): 

I_U ⊗_k k'
LawConflict_i ⊗_k k'
H^n(X, Ob_U) ⊗_k k'

This functoriality turns AAT's relativity into more than silence about the unspoken outside: it is a tool for studying the stability of conclusions when the reading changes.

A.3 Decoration principle

AAT-specific vocabulary never replaces standard geometric objects. Typed architectural data rides on ordinary schemes, locally ringed spaces, derived schemes, and stacks as decoration. The representative decorations are:

At_X
Typed Atom / coordinate labels.
Sig_U
Selected signature axes and readings.
LawRead_U
The selected law universe and witness rules.
I_U
The closed equational obstruction ideal, when defined.
lambda_X
The AAT interpretation map from coordinates to architectural readings.

Decoration never hides the underlying algebraic geometry. A forgetful reading always returns to the standard scheme or stack side.

A.4 Spec_AAT

Spec_AAT is not a new prime spectrum. Given a commutative k-algebra A and an AAT decoration D_A, the decorated affine scheme is 

Spec_AAT(A, D_A)
  :=
  (Spec A, O_{Spec A}, D_A)

where the underlying topological space, the prime ideals, and the structure sheaf are those of the ordinary \( \operatorname{Spec} A \). The AAT-specific information — typed coordinate labels, the selected law reading, signature axis readings, the obstruction ideal, and the interpretation map — is attached as decoration. Consequently, 

point of Spec_AAT(A, D_A)
  =
prime ideal of A
  + selected AAT reading metadata

and the definition of a prime ideal itself is untouched. Morphisms are read the same way: a morphism

\[ \mathrm{Spec}_{\mathrm{AAT}}(B, D_B) \longrightarrow \mathrm{Spec}_{\mathrm{AAT}}(A, D_A) \]

corresponds to an ordinary k-algebra morphism \( A \to B \), together with preservation — or the specified restriction — of typed coordinates, the selected law reading, the obstruction ideal, and the signature reading.

A.5 ArchitectureScheme

An ArchitectureScheme does not carve a new scheme notion away from standard schemes. It is a standard locally ringed space or scheme atlas carrying AAT decoration, read symbolically as 

X_AAT
  =
  (X, O_X, D_X)

where \( (X, \mathcal{O}_X) \) is a standard locally ringed space, called a scheme when its chart atlas is given by ordinary affine schemes, and D_X is the AAT decoration: atom labels, signature readings, law readings, obstruction ideals, and interpretation maps. The affine atlas satisfies 

U_i ≅ Spec A_i

and, as AAT charts,

U_i^AAT ≅ Spec_AAT(A_i, D_i)

Transition maps are open immersions on the underlying scheme side and preserve decoration on the AAT side: 

open immersion:
  U_i ∩ U_j -> U_i

decoration preservation:
  At, Sig, LawRead, I_U, lambda are compatible on overlaps.

cocycle:
  triple overlaps satisfy the usual scheme cocycle condition
  and the selected decoration compatibility.

Under this definition, an ArchitectureScheme is not another name for a ringed topos. The ringed AAT topos is the more general sheaf-theoretic ambient; the ArchitectureScheme is the strong object obtained when an affine chart atlas glues in the standard scheme sense. Gluing affine AAT charts does not in general return the Spec_AAT of the global section ring: 

glue_i Spec_AAT(A_i,D_i)
  need not be
Spec_AAT(Gamma(X,O_X), D_X)

Returning to a single affine chart requires separately assuming that the underlying glued scheme is affine, that the standard comparison morphism with the global section ring is an isomorphism, and that the decoration agrees along that comparison.

A.6 Law conditions as subfunctors

Write the functor of points of an architecture scheme X as

\[ h_X : \mathrm{CommAlg}_k \longrightarrow \mathrm{Set}. \]

The general form of a law condition L is first read as a subfunctor \( L \subset h_X \); when stacky isomorphisms or descent data must be retained, it is read as a substack \( L \subset \mathcal{X} \). This general form removes any need to force every law into a closed ideal:

Closed equational law
Represented by a closed subscheme \( V(I_L) \).
Open law
Represented by an open subscheme \( D(f) \).
Constructible law
Represented by a constructible subfunctor.
Descent law
Represented by a descent condition on local sections.
Temporal law
Represented over a trace topos or trace category.
Stacky law
Represented by a substack or groupoid-valued condition.

The precise form of the main text's slogan is therefore:

closed equational law is equation.
general law is a geometric condition.

A.7 Closed equational laws

A closed equational law L is represented by the ideal generated from its selected witness family, \( I_L \subset \mathcal{O}_X \), and its law locus is the closed subscheme

\[ V(I_L) \subset X. \]

When the law universe U selects a family of closed equational witnesses, the obstruction ideal is read as

\[ I_U = \sum_{L \in U_{\mathrm{closed}}} I_L, \]

and the lawful locus is

\[ \mathrm{Flat}_U(X) = V(I_U). \]

Open, temporal, descent, and stacky laws are handled outside \( \mathrm{Flat}_U(X) \), or as additional subfunctor and substack conditions.

A.8 Presheaf and presentation conditions

For the raw coordinate algebras to genuinely form a presheaf of commutative k-algebras, the structural relation ideal must be stable under restriction. For a context morphism \( i : W' \to W \), when 

res_i(J_struct(W)) subset J_struct(W')

holds, the restriction descends to the quotient algebras: 

O_raw^U(W) -> O_raw^U(W')

Furthermore, to read the sheafified sections O_X^U(W) as the coordinate algebra of the same affine local moduli functor, one separately assumes that the selected finite presentation is not destroyed by sheafification. The main text calls this condition presentation-stability.

A.9 Derived and stacky enhancements

When the closed defect section takes values in a vector bundle or perfect module \( E_U \),

\[ \delta_U \in \Gamma(X, E_U^{\vee}), \]

the lawful locus can be strengthened to a derived zero locus: 

Flat_U^der(X)
  :=
derived zero locus of delta_U

O_{Flat_U^der}
  ≃
Kosz(O_X; delta_U)

The classical lawful locus is read as its truncation:

\[ t_0\, \mathrm{Flat}_U^{\mathrm{der}}(X) = \mathrm{Flat}_U(X). \]

Where isomorphism data is essential — refactor equivalence, decomposition, semantic identification — the object is treated as a groupoid-valued descent object or a stack. The name algebraic architecture stack is used only when diagonal representability, an atlas, and descent of structure hold.

B.2 Label discipline

The labels of the main text are read as follows.

Definition / Construction
Introduces objects and predicates.
Theorem / Proposition / Lemma
A mathematical proposition read under explicitly stated assumptions. Those assuming exactness, coverage, or adequacy are certified bounded inferences.
Theorem candidate
A theorem candidate in the main text with an explicit future definition and proof design. It becomes a central Lean proof obligation only when a corresponding row is placed in the Lean theorem index or the proof obligations ledger.
Principle
A discipline fixing a claim boundary or a way of reading.
Analytic reading
A representation, metric, period, or mass reading of a constructed geometric object.

Thus, for example, Lawfulness–Zero Obstruction is a certified bounded inference relativized to zero-reflecting aggregation and soundness / completeness, not an absolute claim about unselected law universes or metric aggregations. Likewise, the SAGA comparison is a certified bounded inference relativized to the selected finite cover, coefficient data, and comparison data — not an unconditional cohomology identification.

B.3 Finite square-free worked example

Fix a finite context site X as a pseudo-circle cover with two charts A, B and two overlap components P, Q: 

U = {A, B}
A ∩ B = P ⊔ Q

Take the coefficient sheaf to be the constant abelian sheaf \( \mathbb{Z} \), with identity restrictions. The Čech complex of this cover is 

C^0(U,Z) = Z_A ⊕ Z_B
C^1(U,Z) = Z_P ⊕ Z_Q
d(a,b) = (b-a, b-a)

and therefore

\[ H^1(U, \mathbb{Z}) = \big( \mathbb{Z}_P \oplus \mathbb{Z}_Q \big) / \mathrm{diagonal}(\mathbb{Z}) \cong \mathbb{Z}. \]

The cocycle

\[ g = (1, 0) \in \mathbb{Z}_P \oplus \mathbb{Z}_Q \]

is not in the diagonal image, so it gives a nonzero obstruction class. This is the minimal example reading the fact that when local lawful sections carry different mismatches on the two overlap components, they cannot be glued into a global section.

B.4 Square-free obstruction ideal

On the same finite example, place witness variables p, q, r and take the two forbidden supports 

{p,q}
{q,r}

The square-free obstruction ideal is then

\[ I_{\mathrm{Ob}} = \langle pq,\ qr \rangle \subset k[p, q, r]. \]

The corresponding simplicial complex \( \Delta \) is the family of simultaneously admissible witness supports, and

\[ I_{\mathrm{Ob}} = I_{\Delta} \]

is a Stanley-Reisner ideal. The minimal forbidden supports coincide with the generators \( pq \) and \( qr \). The minimal repair hitting sets are the hitting sets of these two forbidden supports, 

{q}
{p,r}

which are the minimal candidates. On the Alexander dual side this reads as: which witnesses must be removed to strike the obstruction pattern.

B.5 Monomial Tor conflict

Read two law universes over the same ambient ring \( R = k[p, q, r] \) as principal monomial ideals: 

I_U = <p q>
I_V = <q r>

Because of the shared witness factor q, the derived intersection is not transverse. The standard principal monomial calculation gives

\[ \mathrm{Tor}_1^R(R/I_U,\ R/I_V) \cong \langle pqr \rangle / \langle pq^2r \rangle, \]

showing that a law conflict residue exists on the support along q. To claim that a concrete repair path triggers a transfer, however, one must separately show that the path meets this support nontrivially, or that the selected transfer pairing returns a nonzero residue.

B.6 Period pairing

Take the generator of \( H^1(U, \mathbb{Z}) \cong \mathbb{Z} \) from Section B.3 to be

\[ \omega = [(1, 0)], \]

and read the formal 1-cycle of overlap components as \( \gamma = P - Q \). The pairing is then

\[ \langle \omega, \gamma \rangle = 1. \]

This value is a period reading relativized to the selected cover, coefficient sheaf, and cycle representative. It is the finite mathematical model of the statement that inside a constructed AAT geometry, a local mismatch class can be read nontrivially along a cycle.

B.8.1 Finite context cover

From this atom family, fix a finite cover with three charts: 

W_dep:
  component / dependency reading.

W_state:
  state transition reading.

W_effect:
  effect / authority reading.

Place the overlap components as

P = W_dep intersection W_state

B.8.2 Čech mismatch

Set the mismatch of local lawful sections to

\[ g = (1, 0, 0) \in \mathbb{Z}_P + \mathbb{Z}_Q + \mathbb{Z}_R, \]

and take the orientation

P : W_dep -> W_state
Q : W_state -> W_effect
R : W_dep -> W_effect

The coboundary of a 0-cochain s = (s_dep, s_state, s_effect) is

\[ d s = (s_{\mathrm{state}} - s_{\mathrm{dep}},\ s_{\mathrm{effect}} - s_{\mathrm{state}},\ s_{\mathrm{effect}} - s_{\mathrm{dep}}). \]

The image of \( d^0 \) is therefore \( \{ (u, v, u + v) \} \), and the functional detecting the class is

\[ g_P + g_Q - g_R. \]

In this example,

\[ 1 + 0 - 0 = 1, \]

so g gives a nonzero class in \( H^1 \). This is the toy model reading the fact that the local dependency, state, and effect readings can each be individually coherent while a mismatch class remains when all three are glued at once.

B.8.3 Obstruction ideal

Place witness variables as

p = dependency-state mismatch witness
q = state-effect mismatch witness
r = dependency-effect mismatch witness

With forbidden supports

{p,q}
{q,r}

one obtains

\[ I_{\mathrm{Ob}} = \langle pq,\ qr \rangle \subset k[p, q, r]. \]

This is the same Stanley-Reisner chart as Section B.4, with witness names assigned from the selected atom family.

B.8.4 Tor and period

Take the law universes

U = dependency-state law
V = state-effect law

and read, over the same ambient ring,

I_U = <p q>
I_V = <q r>

By the shared witness factor q, \( \mathrm{Tor}_1^R(R/I_U, R/I_V) \) carries a derived conflict residue on the support along q. And for the cycle

\[ \gamma = P + Q - R, \]

the period pairing is

\[ \langle g, \gamma \rangle = g_P + g_Q - g_R = 1. \]

This coincides with the functional that detects the Čech class, and it reads the dependency/effect mismatch across the state transition chart.

B.9 Law-equation coefficient and circle nerve worked example

This section fixes the minimal nonzero instance of the SAGA comparison theorem (Part X, Theorem 7.2) on a finite model with a single context, built in the vocabulary of Parts I and II.

B.9.1 Generated coefficient

The finite model has exactly one context, written W. Take the observable ring \( O(W) = \mathbb{Z} \) with identity restriction, exactly one required law, and violation coordinate \( 2 \) at every atom. By the construction of Part III, Theorem 11.4, 

I_L(W) = I_Ob(W) = (2) subset Z

Q(W) = Z/(2) ≅ F_2

The non-degeneracy ledger is:

[1] != 0    (1 not in (2))
[2] = 0

The quotient genuinely separates the lawful reading from the non-lawful reading.

B.9.2 Circle nerve

As the selected simplicial data (Part X, Definition 6.2), fix the following circle nerve: 

vertices: v_-, v_+

edges:
  e_+ : v_- -> v_+
  e_- : v_+ -> v_-

no simplices in degree >= 2

Every chart and overlap is W. This nerve is not generated from chart intersections; it is selected simplicial data over the single-element cover.

B.9.3 Complex and residual

C^0 = Map({v_-, v_+}, F_2) ≅ F_2^2
C^1 = Map({e_+, e_-}, F_2) ≅ F_2^2
C^2 = 0

r(e_+) = [1]
r(e_-) = 0

Then r is a 1-cocycle: the selected complex has no simplices in degree 2, so the cocycle condition holds trivially.

B.9.4 Nonzero class and transfer

Suppose \( r = d^0 p \). The edge \( e_+ \) gives \( p(v_+) - p(v_-) = [1] \), and the edge \( e_- \) gives \( p(v_-) - p(v_+) = 0 \). The latter forces \( p(v_-) = p(v_+) \); substituting into the former yields \( [1] = 0 \), contradicting \( [1] \neq 0 \). Therefore r is not a coboundary.

Read as an invariant: under this orientation, the sum of the two edge values of any coboundary is \( 0 \), while the sum of the two edge values of r is \( [1] \neq 0 \). The nonzeroness reduces to the arithmetic fact of the law-equation quotient, \( 1 \notin (2) \).

Under the semantic coefficient data carried by this complex, the additive regime data, and the identity comparison, 

the residual class of semantic additive H^1 != 0
the class of cover-relative Cech H^1 != 0
the zero-predicate equivalence transfers nonzeroness in both directions

hold (Part X, Theorem 7.4).
