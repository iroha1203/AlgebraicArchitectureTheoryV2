Algebraic Architecture Theory

AAT treats software architecture as a geometric object: generated from atoms, cut out by laws, and read from local to global through obstruction sheaves and cohomology. Local correctness that fails to glue globally is not a mystery in this theory — it is a cohomology class.

The geometry AAT studies

AAT does not study a bare codebase. It studies the relative architecture geometry that appears once four choices are fixed: an atom vocabulary, a law universe, a coverage topology, and a coefficient ring. Atoms are axiomatized as primitive architectural facts. Laws do not create atoms; they impose relations, constraints, and vanishing conditions on atom families. Obstructions witness law failure, and when local witnesses fail to glue, the difference appears as a cohomology class.

Every statement in this edition is read relative to these fixed parameters. When the coefficient ring is not displayed, \( X_C^{V,U,J} \) abbreviates a geometry with \( k \) already fixed.

The central chain

The theory builds one chain of constructions, from primitive facts to a geometry with cohomology, derived structure, measurement, and time.

The chain is read in one direction. Atoms generate; laws cut out; cohomology measures what fails to glue; derived geometry reads how laws conflict; measurement theory bounds what a finite profile can see; evolution geometry and semantic repair descent put the whole picture in motion.

How to read claims

Every claim in this edition is relative to a fixed vocabulary, law universe, coverage topology, coefficient sheaf, and representation family. Three kinds of statements appear, and they are never interchangeable.

Formal theorem
A mathematical proposition proved from the axioms, definitions, and stated assumptions.
Certified bounded inference
A relative inference that holds under explicitly named assumptions such as coverage, exactness, or witness completeness. It is not an unconditional claim.
Analytic reading
A way of reading an already-constructed geometric object through graphs, signatures, periods, or metrics.

A theorem label in this edition is read as a Lean-proved claim only when the Lean status ledger carries a corresponding entry. Every other labeled statement is a mathematical statement of the canonical text, exactly as strong as its stated assumptions.
