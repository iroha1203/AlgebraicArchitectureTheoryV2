Algebraic Architecture Theory

AAT treats software architecture as a geometric object: generated from atoms, cut out by laws, and read from local to global through obstruction sheaves and cohomology. Local correctness that fails to glue globally is not a mystery in this theory — it is a cohomology class.

The geometry AAT studies

AAT does not study a bare codebase. It studies the relative architecture geometry that appears once four choices are fixed: an atom vocabulary, a law universe, a coverage topology, and a coefficient ring. Atoms are axiomatized as primitive architectural facts. Laws do not create atoms; they impose relations, constraints, and vanishing conditions on atom families. Obstructions witness law failure, and when local witnesses fail to glue, the difference appears as a cohomology class.

Every statement in this edition is read relative to these fixed parameters. When the coefficient ring is not displayed, \( X_C^{V,U,J} \) abbreviates a geometry with \( k \) already fixed.

Why algebraic geometry?

The machinery is not a metaphor. Each layer of algebraic geometry answers a question that software architecture actually poses.

Laws arrive as a system, not one at a time
A real architecture is governed by many constraints at once — layering, dependency direction, authority boundaries, contracts — and they hold or fail together. Algebraic geometry is the mathematics of simultaneous equations: law failure is collected into an obstruction ideal, the lawful locus is its common zero set \( \mathrm{Flat}_U(X) = V(\mathcal{I}_{\mathrm{Ob}}^U) \), and interaction between laws becomes ideal arithmetic rather than an informal checklist.
Gluing failure is the central failure
“Every module is correct, yet the whole system breaks” is a descent problem. Sheaf theory turns local sections that do not glue into a precise object, and the mismatch is measured by cohomology: visible failure in \( H^0 \), gluing failure in \( H^1 \), coherence failure in \( H^2 \).
Failure has a grade, not just a flag
An ordinary check reports violation or no violation. Here failure carries structure: the cohomological degree it lives in, the Tor degree of a law conflict, the type of a singularity. “How badly, and in what way, is this broken” becomes a mathematical object — the starting point for prioritizing repair (Part V, Part VI).
A century of instruments applies unchanged
Once architecture is a ringed geometry, existing tools do real work: derived intersections and Tor sheaves read latent law conflict, cotangent complexes read singularities, monodromy reads operation history, stacks and gerbes decide whether a canonical decomposition can exist. None of these had to be invented for software.
The relative point of view is native
Since Grothendieck, algebraic geometry studies objects relative to a base, not absolute ones. AAT’s discipline — fix an atom vocabulary, a law universe, a coverage topology, a coefficient ring, and stay silent about what was not selected — is not a safety clause bolted onto the theory. It is the standard working posture of the mathematics itself.
Geometry admits motion
Because the object is a geometry, further structure can be laid on top of it. Evolution geometry (Part IX) adds the time direction inside AAT, and Software Field Theory reads development as fields and forces over this substrate.

Behind all six answers stands Grothendieck’s image of the rising sea: a problem is not cracked open by force — the ambient theory rises until the problem is surrounded and opens almost by itself. AAT takes the same posture toward software architecture. Cyclic dependencies, hidden coupling, repairs that break what they fix are not attacked one by one as puzzles; the ambient level rises — atoms, sites, ideals, cohomology — until each of them appears as a familiar geometric phenomenon with instruments already attached.

The central chain

The theory builds one chain of constructions, from primitive facts to a geometry with cohomology, derived structure, measurement, and time.

The chain is read in one direction. Atoms generate; laws cut out; cohomology measures what fails to glue; derived geometry reads how laws conflict; measurement theory bounds what a finite profile can see; evolution geometry and semantic repair descent put the whole picture in motion.

How to read claims

Every claim in this edition is relative to a fixed vocabulary, law universe, coverage topology, coefficient sheaf, and representation family. Three kinds of statements appear, and they are never interchangeable.

Formal theorem
A mathematical proposition proved from the axioms, definitions, and stated assumptions.
Certified bounded inference
A relative inference that holds under explicitly named assumptions such as coverage, exactness, or witness completeness. It is not an unconditional claim.
Analytic reading
A way of reading an already-constructed geometric object through graphs, signatures, periods, or metrics.

A theorem label in this edition is read as a Lean-proved claim only when the Lean status ledger carries a corresponding entry. Every other labeled statement is a mathematical statement of the canonical text, exactly as strong as its stated assumptions.

Uses theorem candidates and principles (claim boundaries, ways of reading, or non-claims). Its claims are confined to the selected trace category, state transition presheaf, and temporal coefficient; about events, transitions, and state spaces outside that selection, the text is silent.
Part X
Claims are relativized to the selected charts, overlaps, residual, coefficient, and comparison data, and are read only inside the fixed semantic repair geometry.
