Architecture Geometry: Sites and Sheaves

Part II lifts the architecture object of Part I to a geometric object with local-global structure. Research question

Every module is correct. Every service is correct. Every test passes. Why does the whole system still break?

Part I builds the architecture object from atoms: atom family, configuration, molecule, invariant, law, obstruction, signature, operation. That algebra is generative but not yet local-global. Many architectural failures never appear inside a single local object; they appear only when local views are laid side by side and asked to agree. AAT names this structure exactly:

local sections do not glue globally.

Part II therefore moves from algebra to geometry. It equips the architecture object with local contexts, covers, restrictions, projections, gluing, and descent — the apparatus of a Grothendieck site — so that the local-global gap becomes a mathematical object rather than an anecdote. The chapter builds the stage through sheaves and descent; the law algebra sheaf, obstruction ideal, and lawful locus enter in Part III, and the cohomological reading of descent failure enters in Part IV.

From architecture object to architecture geometry

The architecture object of Part I is generated from an atom configuration: 

F_S^V  ->  Config(F_S^V)  ->  A_S^V

where \( S \) is a source, \( V \) an atom vocabulary, and \( A_S^V \) the resulting architecture object. This object carries many local views — component-local views, feature-local views, semantic slices, runtime slices, boundary slices — and these views overlap, restrict, project, and glue. The object enriched with this local-global structure is the architecture geometry \( X_S^{V,U,J} \), where \( U \) is a law universe and \( J \) a coverage topology.

\( X_S^{V,U,J} \) is a local-global architecture geometry relativized to the atom vocabulary, the law universe, and the coverage topology. Each stage of the diagram is constructed in this chapter.

Architecture geometry

Definition II.2.1 (Architecture Geometry). For a source \( S \), atom vocabulary \( V \), law universe \( U \), and coverage topology \( J \), the architecture geometry is the data

\[ X_S^{V,U,J} = \bigl( A_S^V,\ \mathrm{ArchCtx}(A_S^V),\ J_U \bigr), \]

where \( A_S^V \) is the architecture object generated from the atom family \( F_S^V \), \( \mathrm{ArchCtx}(A_S^V) \) is its category of local contexts, and \( J_U \) is the coverage topology relativized to the law universe \( U \).

Later chapters install further structure on \( X_S^{V,U,J} \):

Part II constructs the stage up to sheaves and descent. The law algebra sheaf, obstruction ideal sheaf, and lawful locus belong to Part III; obstruction cohomology belongs to Part IV.

Principle II.2.2 (Geometry Relativity). Architecture geometry is relativized to the source \( S \), the atom vocabulary \( V \), the law universe \( U \), and the coverage topology \( J \). Different choices give different geometries over the same source: 

X_S^{V,U,J} != X_S^{W,U,J}   in general
X_S^{V,U,J} != X_S^{V,U',J}  in general
X_S^{V,U,J} != X_S^{V,U,J'}  in general

By making explicit which vocabulary generates the atoms, which laws are selected, and which covers read locality, AAT speaks exactly about what it can speak about.

Architecture context

An architecture context is a local view of the architecture geometry — the analogue of an open set.

Definition II.3.1 (Architecture Context). For an architecture object \( A \), an architecture context \( W \in \mathrm{ArchContext}(A) \) is a local reading of \( A \). In the minimal model, a context is readable as the data

\[ W = \bigl( \mathrm{Supp}(W),\ \mathrm{Ax}(W),\ \mathrm{Obs}(W) \bigr), \]

Supp(W)
The atom support read in \( W \).
Ax(W)
The signature and law axes selected in \( W \).
Obs(W)
The family of coordinates, witnesses, boundaries, traces, and semantic data readable in \( W \).

A general context may extend this minimal model. For restriction, projection, refinement, and base change to behave functorially, at least the restriction of support, axes, and observable family must be determined.

A context selects the local view in which the already constructed atom family and architecture object are read. It does not manufacture new architecture.

Example II.3.2 (Local views). The same architecture object yields distinct contexts: 

W_component(C)
  = local view of atoms and relations touching component C

W_feature(F)
  = local view of capability, contract, effect,
    and semantic atoms touching feature F

W_boundary(B)
  = local view containing core/feature, semantic/runtime,
    and authority/effect boundaries

W_runtime(T)
  = local view containing runtime traces, messages,
    retries, timeouts, transactions

These carry different information, yet all are local views of one architecture object.

Architecture context category

Definition II.4.1 (Architecture Context Category). For an architecture object \( A \), the architecture context category \( \mathrm{ArchCtx}(A) \) has architecture contexts as objects and re-readings between contexts as morphisms \( i : W' \to W \). In this orientation \( W' \) is a readable local context over \( W \): local contexts map into global contexts.

Morphisms arise as restriction, projection, refinement, forgetting, embedding, and base change. A morphism expresses which information the passage between local views preserves, which it forgets, and which it reads more finely. In the minimal model, a morphism \( i : W' \to W \) is a readable restriction or refinement satisfying 

Supp(W') maps into or refines Supp(W)
Ax(W')   maps into or refines Ax(W)
Obs(W)   restricts functorially to Obs(W')

so context morphisms induce coordinate restriction and witness restriction, and overlaps are constructed as pullbacks or intersections of supports, axes, and observable families. When the category is used as a minimal poset site, one sets \( W' \le W \) iff \( W' \) is a local or refined readable context of \( W \). This poset model already suffices as the base example for sheaves, Čech complexes, and affine chart gluing in later parts.

Proposition II.4.2

Minimal Context Finite-Meet Site

proved
Statement
In the minimal context model, if supports, axes, and observable families have finite meets compatible with observable restriction, morphisms are unique (\( \mathrm{Hom}(W',W) \) empty or contractible), and context equality is taken up to readable-identity equivalence, then \( \mathrm{ArchCtx}_{\min}(A) \) is a finite-meet poset category. Overlaps are meets: \( W_i \times_W W_j = W_i \wedge_W W_j \).
Proof idea
Thinness comes from morphism uniqueness; antisymmetry holds after the equivalence quotient; identities are readable identities and composition is composition of restrictions/refinements. Without the quotient the same data is a finite-meet preorder category.

Observable families are read through pullbacks of the restriction diagram, not bare set intersections: the meet of two contexts keeps exactly the observations both contexts can still read compatibly. Assumption II.4.3 (Pullback and Overlap). \( \mathrm{ArchCtx}(A) \) is assumed to have the pullbacks needed to form cover overlaps: 

W_i -> W
W_j -> W
----------------
W_i x_W W_j

This pullback is the context overlap. The Čech complex of Part IV is defined against these overlaps.

Principle II.4.4 (Context Non-Generation). Contexts do not create atoms. Context morphisms, restrictions, projections, and refinements do not create atoms. Atom existence belongs to the atom axiom system of Part I; a context only chooses where to read.

Restriction, projection, refinement, base change

The morphisms of the context category control locality in AAT. Four roles are singled out.

Restriction

Restriction passes from a larger context to a smaller one, restricting the atoms, laws, signatures, state, effects, and semantic facts visible in the local view:

\[ \mathrm{res}_{W,W'} : F(W) \to F(W'), \]

where \( W' \) is a local part of \( W \).

Projection

Projection keeps the selected coordinates and forgets the others, \( \mathrm{proj} : W_{\mathrm{axis}} \to W \), where \( W_{\mathrm{axis}} \) reads \( W \) along the selected axes. An axis that becomes invisible under projection has not become zero; it has been forgotten. AAT keeps this distinction as a standing discipline: 

forgotten   != zero
unobserved  != zero
unmeasured  != zero

Refinement

Refinement decomposes a coarse context into finer ones, \( \mathrm{refine} : W' \to W \) — for instance, a module-level view into function-level views or runtime trace slices. Refinement makes facts already present in the canonical atom family visible in a finer context; it does not create facts.

Base change

Base change pulls a cover or local data on one context back along a morphism to another: 

W' -> W
{ U_i -> W }
----------------
{ U_i x_W W' -> W' }

For the coverage topology to be a Grothendieck topology, covers must be stable under this base change. Section 7 closes the axioms by generation.

Coverage families

Definition II.6.1 (Coverage Family). For a context \( W \), a coverage family is a family of local contexts

\[ \{\, W_i \to W \,\}_{i \in I}, \]

expressing that the required support, law witnesses, boundaries, and signature axes of \( W \) are read through the local views \( W_i \). What counts as "covered" is relativized to the law universe and the selected reading, per Principle II.7.3.

Example II.6.2 (Readings of covers).

Component cover
Covers the required component support with component-local contexts.
Feature cover
Covers a feature extension with feature-local contexts and core contexts.
Boundary cover
Explicitly covers core/feature overlaps, semantic/runtime overlaps, and authority/effect overlaps.
Runtime cover
Covers interactions invisible to static views by runtime trace slices.

AAT Grothendieck topology

For a law universe \( U \), AAT first defines admissible covers on \( \mathrm{ArchCtx}(A) \), then takes the Grothendieck topology they generate as the AAT coverage topology \( J_U \).

Definition II.7.1 (AAT Grothendieck Topology). The assignment \( J_U^{\mathrm{adm}}(W) \) collects the admissible coverage families of each context \( W \). A family \( \{ W_i \to W \} \) is admissible when it satisfies at least the five conditions:

These semantic coverage conditions do not by themselves imply base-change stability or transitivity. The AAT coverage topology is therefore defined as the smallest Grothendieck topology generated by the admissible covers:

\[ J_U = \text{the Grothendieck topology generated by } J_U^{\mathrm{adm}}. \]

In particular \( J_U \) contains:

identity / isomorphism:
  identities and isomorphisms are covers.

base-change stability:
  covers remain covers under pullback / refinement.

transitivity:
  covers of covers are covers.

This generation closes the site axioms on the topology side even when law witness coverage or boundary coverage can be lost after refinement. Where a required witness disappears in a refined cover, that is not a failure of the cover; it reappears as a failure of coverage or witness completeness in the hypotheses of later theorems. All sheaves on the AAT site are read against this generated topology \( J_U \).

U-adequate covers and witness closure

Definition II.7.2 (U-Adequate Cover). Among the covers of the generated topology \( J_U \), a cover \( \mathcal{U} = \{ W_i \to W \} \) is \( U \)-adequate when it is a \( J_U \)-cover and, for the selected data a theorem requires, satisfies: 

required atom support is covered.
required law witnesses appear on patches or overlaps.
required signature axes are readable on the cover.
required boundary witnesses are visible on overlaps.
restriction maps preserve the selected witness ideals.

\( J_U \) is the topology for sheaf theory; \( U \)-adequacy is the additional predicate for reading laws, witnesses, and axes. Theorems about lawfulness and cohomological flatness in later parts quantify over \( U \)-adequate covers.

Lemma II.7.2A · Certified bounded inference

Witness-Closure Cover

proved
Statement
Suppose the required witness family is locally finite, each witness support is representable in the context category, and the needed finite intersections or overlaps exist. Suppose further, for the selected reading, that restriction maps preserve the selected witness ideals, required signature axes are readable on the closed cover, and required boundary witnesses remain visible on overlaps. Then the witness-closure cover — the cover closed up to contain the required witness supports and their boundary overlaps — is \( U \)-adequate.
Proof idea
Closing the cover over the finitely many required witness supports and their overlaps realizes support, witness, axis, and boundary coverage directly; the ideal-preservation, axis-readability, and boundary-visibility hypotheses supply the remaining adequacy clauses.

Principle II.7.3 (Coverage Relativity). Coverage is relativized to the law universe and the selected reading: covered within \( U \), covered for the selected witnesses, covered for the selected axes. About atoms and axes outside the coverage, AAT asserts neither zero nor lawful.

The finite poset regime

For finite poset contexts, covers by principal contexts are the tractable standard example. Sheaves on a poset are read as functors, sections over principal contexts behave like stalks, and for many coefficients the Čech computation reduces to finite linear algebra, with nonzero cohomological degrees bounded above by the depth of the context hierarchy.

Definition II.7.2B (Finite Poset AAT Site Regime). An AAT site is in the finite poset AAT site regime when: 

ArchCtx_min(A) is a finite poset category.
finite meets give selected overlaps.
J_U is generated by finite witness-closure covers.
selected coefficient sheaves are functors on this finite poset.
restriction maps preserve selected witness ideals and axes.

Cover nerves, overlaps, and restrictions are induced from atom supports, axes, and observable families.

Proposition II.7.2C

Finite Poset Computation

proved
Statement
In the finite poset regime, for a finite witness-closure cover \( \mathcal{U} = \{ W_i \to W \} \) and a coefficient sheaf \( F \) Čech-computable on it, the cover-relative Čech complex \[ C^n(\mathcal{U}, F) = \prod_{\substack{\text{nonempty } (n+1)\text{-fold overlaps}}} F\bigl(W_{i_0 \dots i_n}\bigr) \] is a finite complex, so each \( H^n(\mathcal{U}, F) \) is computed from finitely many section groups and restriction maps. If the cover nerve has dimension \( d \), then \( C^n(\mathcal{U},F) = 0 \) and \( H^n(\mathcal{U},F) = 0 \) for \( n > d \).
Proof idea
Finiteness of the poset and of the cover index bounds the nonempty overlaps; above the nerve dimension there are no nonempty \( (n+1) \)-fold overlaps, so the cochain groups themselves vanish.

AAT site

Definition II.8.1 (AAT Site). For an architecture object \( A_S^V \), a law universe \( U \), and the coverage topology \( J_U \), the AAT site is the pair

\[ \mathrm{Site}_{\mathrm{AAT}}(S,V,U,J) = \bigl( \mathrm{ArchCtx}(A_S^V),\ J_U \bigr), \]

abbreviated \( \mathrm{Site}_{\mathrm{AAT}}(A,U,J) = (\mathrm{ArchCtx}(A), J_U) \) when the architecture object is fixed. Locality, coverage, projection, unmeasured axes, and gluing in AAT are all controlled by this site.

Architecture sheaves

When a presheaf satisfies the sheaf condition, local data glues uniquely.

Definition II.10.1 (Sheaf Condition). A presheaf \( F \) is a sheaf when, for every cover \( \{ W_i \to W \} \), compatible local sections glue to a unique global section:

exists unique s in F(W)

Gluing and descent

Gluing and descent are the conditions under which local data determines global data — the technical heart of the chapter's opening question.

Definition II.11.1 (Gluing Data). For a cover \( \{ W_i \to W \} \), gluing data is a family of local sections \( s_i \in F(W_i) \). The family is compatible on overlaps when

\[ s_i|_{W_{ij}} = s_j|_{W_{ij}}. \]

Definition II.11.2 (Descent). A presheaf \( F \) satisfies descent for a cover \( \{ W_i \to W \} \) when compatible gluing data comes from a global section: 

local sections
+ agreement on overlaps
+ cocycle condition
--------------------------------
global section

When descent fails, data that looks consistent locally does not glue into global architecture data. This is the exact shape of the opening failure mode:

local lawful does not imply global lawful.

Each module can carry a lawful local section while no global section exists over them. The cohomological reading of this failure — where the obstruction to gluing lives and how it is measured — is the subject of Part IV.

Sheafification gap

A raw presheaf \( F_{\mathrm{raw}} : \mathrm{ArchCtx}(A)^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathbf{Set} \) is in general not a sheaf. Sheafification produces \( F_{\mathrm{raw}}^{+} \) with a canonical map

\[ F_{\mathrm{raw}} \to F_{\mathrm{raw}}^{+}. \]

Definition II.12.1 (Sheafification Gap). The sheafification gap is the difference between the raw local data and the sheafified data: \( \mathrm{Gap}(F_{\mathrm{raw}}) \) is the difference between \( F_{\mathrm{raw}} \) and \( F_{\mathrm{raw}}^{+} \).

In AAT, the gap is read as:

missing overlap evidence
projection loss
coverage gap
incompatible local readings
failed gluing
unmeasured axis

The sheafification gap is the local-global deficiency that precedes obstruction cohomology: it records where raw architectural readings already fail to be sheaf-like, before any cohomology class is computed.

Toward the AAT topos

From the AAT site, the sheaf category follows:

\[ \mathrm{AATSh}(A,U,J) = \mathrm{Sh}\bigl( \mathrm{ArchCtx}(A),\ J_U \bigr). \]

This is the sheaf category of AAT. Part III places the law algebra sheaf \( \mathcal{O}_X^U \) on it and defines the obstruction ideal sheaf \( \mathcal{I}_{\mathrm{Ob}}^U \) and the lawful locus \( \mathrm{Flat}_U(X) \).

With this, AAT passes from an atom-generated algebra into a Grothendieck-style architecture geometry with genuine local-global structure.

Worked example: the two-patch finite site

The Lean development fires the Part II stage on a concrete finite model. On the Part I finite model, a selected four-context poset carries a two-patch adequate cover with an explicit overlap: two patch contexts over a base, with overlap-to-patch morphisms realizing \( W_1 \times_W W_2 \) as a meet. On this site:

  • Descent fires The unit sheaf satisfies the sheaf condition and descent on the two-patch cover (twoPatchUnit_isSheaf, twoPatchUnit_descent).
  • The gap is exhibited A selected sheafification gap is constructed on the same cover (twoPatchSheafificationGap), showing raw data that the sheafified object separates.
  • Čech computes The selected nerve is one-dimensional, the Boolean-coefficient Čech complex vanishes above degree one (twoPatchFinitePosetRegime_cech_vanishes_above_one), and a separated zero-cochain has a nonzero differential (twoPatchSeparatedCochain_differential_nonzero) — a finite, fully checked instance of local sections failing to agree on an overlap.
  • The topology bridge fires On the singleton equality-thin site, the generated topology coincides with Mathlib's Coverage.toGrothendieck under concrete pullback and base-change-stability instances (siteTopology_eq_coverage_toGrothendieck).

The example is deliberately small. Its role is to show that every constructor of the chapter — context, morphism, meet, cover, adequacy, sheaf condition, descent, gap, Čech complex — is inhabited and computes on finite data.

Counterexamples and non-conclusions

The chapter's claim boundary is as important as its constructions. Three disciplines recur.

Projection is not zero. An axis invisible after projection is forgotten, not measured to be zero. A dependency view that omits the authority axis says nothing about authority; the site keeps forgotten != zero as a structural fact of the morphism, not a caveat.

Coverage is relative. Per Principle II.7.3, "covered" means covered within \( U \), for the selected witnesses and axes. About atoms and axes outside the coverage, the theory asserts neither zero nor lawful — it is silent.

Local lawful does not imply global lawful. Descent can fail: each patch carries a lawful section while no global section exists. This is a theorem-shaped fact about the site, exhibited on the finite model by a nonzero degree-zero differential, and its systematic measurement is Part IV's obstruction cohomology.

Non-conclusions. Part II does not claim unconditional base-change stability of arbitrary precoverages, does not compute general sheaf cohomology from cover-relative Čech cohomology without a separately fixed Leray or acyclicity assumption, and does not construct the law algebra sheaf — that construction belongs to Part III.

Chapter summary

Part II gives AAT its geometry. Local views of an architecture object form a context category; admissible covers generate a Grothendieck topology; the pair is the AAT site. Local data lives in presheaves, glues in sheaves, and descends — or fails to descend — along covers. The opening question now has a precise home: "every module is correct, yet the whole breaks" is the statement that compatible local sections admit no global section, and the sheafification gap and descent failure locate exactly where the local-global passage breaks before any cohomology is computed.
