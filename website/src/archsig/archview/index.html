The projection layer

ArchSig measures. ArchView shows you where it doesn't close.

ArchView is a no-build, single-file 3D viewer for ArchSig output. It draws the measured cover as patches, declared values as sheets floating above them, and the measured mismatch as an amber seam you can click — landing on the exact source path and line. It adds no verdict of its own.

Live demo: the one-cent drift, three frames

The viewer below is loaded with three real, committed archsig analyze runs of the shipped demo — base, head, repaired — as a measured sequence. Use the timeline at the bottom to step between frames; the camera holds still so the change you see is the change that was measured. A gate report can be supplied separately to the final SAGA stage.

  • Open the live demoOpens in a new tab. Loads Three.js from a CDN; needs network access.

What to look for: in the head frame, the settlement cycle appears and the Global consistency scene shows an amber seam on the edges that carry the mismatch — application↔settlement, infrastructure↔ports, infrastructure↔settlement. Switch to the Section lanes scene and the declared money conventions appear as three sheet fragments that refuse to bridge. In the repaired frame the seam is gone and a single teal sheet covers every patch: a global section exists, and you are looking at it.

Then open the SAGA diagnostic staircase scene and step through the same three frames. In base the whole staircase is grey: the SAGA questions were asked, but no RepairPlan was supplied, so every stage is typed silence. In head the staircase is measured end to end — grounding teal (every chart's own money law holds), descent amber (the residual class in Z1/B1 is nonzero), comparison teal (the supplied cochain-map transport checked out) — with the harmonic-debt reading riding on the descent stage. In repaired the descent stage turns teal: the residual glues within the selected complex. The staircase is the demo's argument drawn as geometry: every module right, the loop wrong, the repair measured.

How to read the picture

The geometry is the AAT picture drawn literally, and every visual channel is driven by a measured quantity:

  • Patches are contextsEach bounded context is a translucent disk. Position comes from a spectral embedding of the measured nerve — contexts that share more structure sit closer. Patch size scales with evidence atom count.
  • Lenses are overlapsWhere two contexts overlap, a vesica-shaped lens holds the shared atoms; lens size scales with the shared-atom count, and its rim thickens with mismatch markers.
  • Sheets are declared valuesAbove the base, each declared section value floats as a sheet fragment over its patches. Where values agree across an overlap the sheets bridge; where they disagree the sheet is cut by a zigzag fault line.
  • The amber seam is the measurementOnly the measured H¹ cocycle support ignites amber (with the only glow in the scene). Four color lanes are reserved: teal = measured zero, amber = measured nonzero, lavender = analytic reading, grey = silence. Nothing else uses saturated color.

Silence is drawn as fog and frosted glass, never as red error. An unmeasured region does not look safe and does not look broken — it looks unobserved, which is what it is.

Each scene answers one question. The names below are the Engineer-terms lane; a toolbar chip switches every label to the mathematical vocabulary without changing any claim.

Three honesty features

  • Fidelity declarationClick any element and the detail panel declares, per visual channel, whether it is measured (driven by a measured quantity), layout (an embedding of measured adjacency — positions carry no verdict), or decoration (bloom, pulses). A toolbar Decoration toggle strips the decoration entirely; the claims do not change.
  • Two vocabulary lanesEngineer terms by default, mathematical terms on a toggle. Only the wording changes — the conclusion codes, boundaries, and claims are identical in both lanes.
  • Click-to-source landingAtoms and amber seams resolve to path:line (plus symbol) from the ArchMap's source declarations. On an amber element you get the conflicting section declarations side by side, each with its code location — the screen-to-editor path for fixing the actual mismatch. ArchView displays locations resolved by ArchSig; it never invents one.

On your own output

ArchView is one HTML file that reads archsig-atom-viewer-data.json (plus the summary, manifest, and optional archsig-gate-report.json) from its own directory. After any analyze run:

You can also drag any viewer-data JSON onto an open viewer, then choose Open gate report… for the second input. The report is shown only when its schema, action vocabulary, and measurement-packet digest match the loaded viewer-data; otherwise the status line explains the rejection. If an archview-sequence.json manifest sits beside the viewer — as in the demo above — it plays the declared frames as a measured sequence, keeping every declared slot visible even when a frame fails to load. The viewer uses ES modules and loads Three.js from a CDN, so it must be served over HTTP (not opened as a bare file) and needs network access.

Boundary

ArchView is a projection, strictly downstream of measurement. It draws what the viewer-data artifact contains and stays silent about everything else: no new verdicts, no inferred positions, no completion of missing evidence. H² coherence is not visualized (triple overlaps appear as existence-only frosted faces), and near-flat geometry is not presented as lawfulness. If the picture and the packet ever seem to disagree, the packet is the measurement — and the fidelity declaration exists so you can tell which parts of the picture are measurement at all.
