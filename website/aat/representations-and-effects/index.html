Can algebraic architecture objects generate graph, matrix, analytic, and effect representations?

Graph and matrix

On a finite unweighted universe, acyclicity, absence of closed walks, and nilpotence of the adjacency matrix can express the same static structure. Finite propagation is not baked into that structural theorem; it depends on a walk universe and a propagation model.

Definition 13.1. The graph / matrix bridge is the selected finite structural reading that compares an unweighted dependency graph, its finite walk evidence, and its adjacency matrix. Its input is a proof-carrying finite component universe, not an ambient repository.

Proposition 13.2. For a finite selected unweighted structural universe, the graph and matrix readings are theorem-connected: acyclicity, absence of nonempty closed walks, and adjacency nilpotence are the same structural zero package, with the spectral-radius reading living on the corresponding finite complex matrix.

Proof idea. Powers of the finite adjacency matrix enumerate selected finite walks. If the graph is acyclic, sufficiently long selected walks cannot exist, so a positive matrix power is zero. Conversely, a nonempty closed walk can be repeated past any nilpotence cutoff and refutes matrix nilpotence. The spectral statement is then read through the finite complex adjacency matrix; it does not attach cost or runtime meaning to the radius.

Non-conclusion 13.3. Decomposable remains StrictLayered. Acyclicity, finite propagation, nilpotence, and spectral conditions are separate structural or analytic readings rather than part of that base definition.

Analytic representation

AnalyticRepresentation maps a structural architecture object into an analytic domain. The representation can be zero-preserving, zero-reflecting, obstruction-preserving, or obstruction-reflecting. These are different strengths; reflecting directions carry stronger evidence obligations.

Definition 13.4. An AnalyticRepresentation consists of a representation map together with separate predicates for preserving and reflecting selected zeros and obstructions. The reflecting predicates carry coverage, witness completeness, semantic contract coverage, and non-conclusion fields.

  • ZeroPreserving Structural zero implies analytic zero.
  • ZeroReflecting Analytic zero plus assumptions implies structural zero.
  • ObstructionPreserving Structural obstruction implies analytic obstruction.
  • ObstructionReflecting An analytic obstruction can be read back as structural through explicit assumptions.

Representation pattern

A representation theorem has two directions with different obligations. Preserving sends a structural zero or witness into the representation. Reflecting reads a represented zero or obstruction back into the structural world through stronger assumptions.

This pattern also explains why dashboards and numerical summaries are useful but insufficient: an aggregate can guide attention without by itself proving structural flatness.

Proposition 13.5. Preserving and reflecting are independent theorem boundaries. A preserving representation can transport a structural zero into an analytic zero; a reflecting claim additionally needs the recorded assumptions that authorize reading the analytic result back as a selected structural claim.

Proof idea. The Lean API keeps the four predicates separate. The preserving theorems use the corresponding preserving field; the reflecting theorems use the reflecting field and its assumptions. A report with a represented value still needs the reflecting package to discharge the structural theorem.

Valuation boundary

ObstructionValuation maps selected witnesses to numeric or ordered values. Aggregates can be useful for dashboards; an aggregate zero reflects witness-level zero through a zero-reflecting value domain and aggregation under the selected assumptions.

This is the same boundary discipline as matrix and analytic representations: preserving zero is weaker than reflecting zero, and reflecting an obstruction requires coverage and semantic contract assumptions.

Proposition 13.6. A zero valuation discharges selected witness absence when its zero-reflecting premise is available. An optional missing value, an unavailable axis, or an unmeasured report field is a different kind of zero.

State transitions and effects

The state-transition layer treats commands, events, retries, compensations, snapshots, CRUD overwrites, and migrations as generators, with laws expressed as relations. Event Sourcing, Saga, CRUD, retry safety, snapshot consistency, and migration naturality are projections of this algebra.

The effect boundary captures obstruction that a dependency graph alone may miss: cross-boundary effects, implicit authority, missing handlers, and non-commuting effect pairs.

Definition 14.1. A state-transition or effect-boundary law package is a selected finite family of replay, roundtrip, compensation, projection, migration, or effect relations read against a carrier and observation semantics. Lawfulness is a package claim over those selected relations.

  • Event Sourcing Replay and projection laws are selected finite law cases; complete real-code event logs require separate evidence.
  • Saga Compensation and weak recovery are selected law packages; general inverses for failure modes require separate assumptions.
  • CRUD Overwrite behavior is read against the selected state and observation relation, separate from broad claims about CRUD.
  • Circuit Breaker Runtime protection is relative to selected localization and failure-model assumptions.
  • Replicated Log Convergence is conditional on selected quorum, ordering, failure model, and entries.

Effect examples

A dependency graph may show that checkout depends only on a payment interface, while the effect layer reveals a hidden authority boundary: a retry command is not idempotent, a compensation does not undo the action, a snapshot omits a required event, or a migration fails to commute with the old projection.

  • Replay law Projection after appending events agrees with replaying the prior projection through those events.
  • Compensation law A compensation sequence is observationally equivalent to the relevant identity behavior.
  • Migration naturality Old and new projections commute with the migration relation under stated assumptions.
  • Effect obstruction Missing handlers, implicit authority, or non-commuting effect pairs can be selected obstruction witnesses.

Proposition 14.2. State-transition and effect-boundary lawfulness can be connected to selected obstruction absence for the law family and observation semantics supplied to the package.

Proof idea. The law family packages expose the selected laws and their obstruction predicates in the same universe. The bridge theorem states that selected family lawfulness is equivalent to absence of the selected family obstruction. Runtime traces, hidden effects, and real-code extractor completeness require their own evidence packages.

Chapter summary

Representations let AAT move from algebraic architecture objects into graphs, matrices, signature axes, and state-transition law families. Preservation carries selected structure forward; reflection reads represented evidence back through the needed assumptions.

The effect layer extends the same discipline to commands, events, replay, compensation, projection, and migration. A static dependency view can therefore be connected to semantic and effect readings while each representation keeps its own assumptions.

Terms

representation
A map from selected structural evidence into another domain such as a graph, matrix, signature axis, or state-transition law family.
preserving direction
A structural zero or obstruction is sent into a represented zero or obstruction.
reflecting direction
A represented zero or obstruction is read back as structural through explicit assumptions.
measured zero
The selected axis was measured and its in-scope value is zero.
unmeasured axis
The axis is not available in the selected evidence; it is not a zero.
state-transition algebra
The command, event, replay, compensation, projection, and migration laws read as selected finite law cases.
effect boundary
The selected law surface for effects that a static dependency graph may not see.
