Part I. Foundations

Design Laws and Invariants

A design law is not a slogan and not an atom. It is a selected predicate over molecules: it evaluates finite configurations of already-existing atoms, marks bad configurations, and lets lawfulness be stated relative to a law universe.

Research question

How can design principles be stated precisely enough to test an architecture without turning them into universal slogans?

AAT treats a design principle as a law only after selecting the molecules, invariants, witness family, coverage, and exactness assumptions needed to evaluate it. A law is therefore not the principle's name. It is the theorem-shaped condition that says which finite atom configurations are acceptable under a chosen reading.

Law definition

A design law evaluates molecules. In the pure Lean surface, a law marks selected molecules as bad; a molecule is lawful for that law exactly when it is not marked bad.

Definition 1.1. A DesignLaw is a predicate-level evaluator for atom molecules. It classifies selected configurations; it does not add atoms, remove atoms, or define the atom ontology.

Proposition 1.2. Law evaluation is downstream from Atom and Molecule. A law can say that a molecule is bad, but it cannot make a primitive fact exist.

Proof. Lean exposes this through AAT.DesignLaw.does_not_create_atoms, which returns system.noLawCreatesAtoms. It also proves AAT.DesignLaw.atom_law_independent, showing that selected design laws do not change the law-independence of root atoms.

Law universe

A single law rarely captures a design principle. A law universe is a selected family of laws together with the assumptions that make them readable: required laws, optional laws, derived laws, witness families, selected readings, coverage assumptions, and exactness assumptions.

Definition 2.1. A law universe is the selected family of law predicates used to read an architecture object. It is relative to a molecule boundary, a witness boundary, and the evidence that covers that boundary.

Non-conclusion 2.2. A law universe is not "all good design." It is a declared package. Passing one package does not automatically discharge laws outside that package.

Invariants

Invariants are the quantities or predicates that a law protects. A design law may require an invariant to be preserved, a forbidden witness count to remain zero, a projection to be sound, a substitution to preserve a contract, or an operation to improve a selected measure.

Definition 3.1. An invariant family is a selected collection of properties or quantities that a law package preserves, tests, reflects, or improves.

Proposition 3.2. Invariants are law-relative. Cycle count, contract mismatch count, projection violation count, replay failure count, and semantic inconsistency count are different quantities, not one universal quality score.

Proof idea. Each invariant reads a different projection of the architecture object. A static dependency invariant can be preserved while an effect-ordering invariant fails. A local substitution invariant can hold while a global layering invariant fails. Therefore AAT keeps invariant families separated by law package.

Design principle layers

Design principles become useful in AAT when their layer is made explicit. SOLID-style principles usually live in local contract and substitution layers. Layered and Clean Architecture live in global structure layers. Event Sourcing, Saga, and CRUD live in state-transition algebra layers. Circuit Breaker lives in runtime dependency layers. Replicated Log lives in distributed convergence layers.

SOLID
Local contracts, abstraction, substitutability, interface separation, dependency inversion.
Layered / Clean Architecture
Ranking, dependency direction, inner/outer separation, abstraction consistency, global structure.
Event Sourcing / Saga / CRUD
Replay, projection, compensation, overwrite, history, state-transition algebra.
Circuit Breaker
Runtime protection, failure locality, timeout, retry, bulkhead and fallback behavior.
Replicated Log
Ordering, quorum, failure model, conditional convergence, distributed consistency.

Proposition 4.1. Design principle layers do not automatically imply one another. Local contract success does not imply global decomposability, state-transition safety, runtime resilience, or distributed convergence.

Proof idea. Each layer evaluates different atom families and different invariants. A class can satisfy a local interface contract while its dependencies violate a layer ranking. A saga can preserve a compensation invariant while a replicated log fails a quorum condition. The layers can interact, but an explicit theorem is needed to move from one to another.

Patterns as law packages

AAT does not read SOLID, GoF patterns, DDD patterns, or resilience patterns as badges attached to code. It reads them as compact names for law packages: selected atom families, invariants, allowed operations, forbidden molecules, and witness rules. The familiar pattern name is useful, but the AAT object is the law package behind it.

This changes how pattern discussion works. Instead of asking whether a design "uses Strategy" or "follows DIP", AAT asks which molecules the pattern selects, which invariants it preserves, which substitutions or dependencies are forbidden, and which counterexample would witness failure.

Single Responsibility Principle
A law package over change reasons, capability grouping, state ownership, and effect locality. A failure witness is not "a large class" but a molecule where independent change axes are forced through the same subject.
Open/Closed Principle
A preservation law for extension operations. The selected invariant is that new behavior factors through declared extension points without rewriting protected molecules.
Liskov Substitution Principle
A contract and semantic preservation law. Type compatibility is only one atom family; substitution also has to preserve contract, effect, authority, and semantic atoms selected by the law package.
Interface Segregation Principle
A projection law over capability and dependency atoms. A client should depend on the capability molecule it actually uses, not on an unrelated bundle of capabilities.
Dependency Inversion Principle
A relation and contract factoring law. Concrete dependencies should factor through selected abstraction atoms, and the abstraction must carry the contract atoms required by the client.
Strategy / Adapter / Facade
Role interpretations over molecules. Strategy emphasizes substitutable behavior contracts, Adapter emphasizes translation between contract surfaces, and Facade emphasizes projection of a larger molecule into a smaller capability surface.
Repository
A state and contract law package. It hides persistence details behind selected capability and contract atoms, but it does not automatically prove transaction safety, query semantics, or consistency.
Saga
A state-transition and effect-compensation law package. Its witnesses live in ordering, compensation, idempotence, and semantic completion atoms, not merely in the presence of event handlers.
Circuit Breaker
A runtime interaction law package. It reads timeout, failure count, open/half-open state, fallback, and effect-locality atoms. It does not by itself prove semantic correctness of the fallback.

Proposition 5.1. A design pattern becomes theorem-shaped in AAT when its pattern name is replaced by a selected law package: atom support, invariant family, allowed operations, forbidden molecules, and witness criteria.

Proof idea. A pattern name alone has no truth value over an architecture object. The truth value appears only after selecting the molecules and predicates it evaluates. Once those are selected, the pattern can be read as a DesignLaw, a family of invariants, or a law universe with explicit witness rules.

Non-conclusion 5.2. AAT does not turn famous patterns into universal laws. It turns them into explicit selected laws whose scope, evidence, and failure witnesses can be checked.

Lawfulness

Lawfulness is selected. An architecture object is lawful relative to a selected law universe when every required law in that universe holds on the selected molecule boundary. AAT does not identify lawfulness with zero obstruction until the required witness family, coverage, exactness, and bridge assumptions have been supplied.

Proposition 6.1. Lawfulness and obstruction absence require a bridge. They are equivalent only when bad-witness completeness and circuit soundness are supplied for the selected molecule boundary.

Proof. Lean packages this as AAT.LawfulnessBridge. The theorem LawfulnessBridge.lawful_iff_no_obstructionCircuit proves equivalence between LawfulWithinMoleculeConfiguration and NoRequiredObstructionCircuit using badWitnessComplete and circuitBad. Without those bridge assumptions, the equivalence is not licensed.

Worked example

Consider the payment molecule from the previous chapter. A payment law package may select several laws over the same atom surface.

A static graph reading of this molecule may pass even when the runtime retry law fails. A contract law may pass while the authority law fails. AAT keeps those results separate until a selected law universe states exactly which laws are required together.

Counterexamples and non-conclusions

Counterexample 7.1. A module can satisfy an interface segregation law and still participate in a forbidden dependency cycle. Local contract lawfulness does not imply global structure lawfulness.

Counterexample 7.2. A payment effect can have an authority atom and still fail an idempotence contract under retry. Authority lawfulness does not imply effect replay lawfulness.

Counterexample 7.3. A law can mark no selected molecule bad because the selected universe omitted the relevant witness. That is not global lawfulness; it is a coverage limitation.

Non-conclusion 7.4. Passing SOLID-style local laws does not prove Clean Architecture, Event Sourcing correctness, Saga compensation, Circuit Breaker resilience, replicated-log convergence, or semantic correctness unless those laws are included and bridged.

Formal reading

The pure Lean surface for this chapter is small and deliberate: AAT.DesignLaw evaluates molecules by a Bad predicate, DesignLaw.Lawful means not bad, and accessors record that laws do not create atom existence. The obstruction bridge lives in Formal/Arch/AAT/Circuit.lean and is developed in the next chapter.

Formal status 8.1. DesignLaw and DesignLaw.Lawful are defined in Lean. The accessors proving law non-generation and atom law-independence are proved.

Formal status 8.2. The lawfulness-to-obstruction bridge is proved when the bridge assumptions are supplied. The concrete catalog of SOLID, layered architecture, saga, circuit breaker, and replicated log laws remains a law-package formalization target.

Chapter summary

AAT turns design principles into selected law packages over atom molecules. A law evaluates an existing molecule; it does not generate atoms. Invariants say what the law protects, and law universes say which laws are being considered together.

The key discipline is layer separation. Local contract laws, global structure laws, state-transition laws, runtime dependency laws, and distributed convergence laws can support each other only through explicit theorems. Without those theorems, success in one layer is not success in another.

Terms

DesignLaw
A molecule evaluator that marks selected configurations as bad.
Lawful
The selected molecule is not bad for the selected law.
Law universe
A selected package of required, optional, and derived laws with witness and evidence boundaries.
Invariant family
The selected quantities or predicates a law preserves, tests, reflects, or improves.
Coverage
The evidence includes the molecules or witnesses needed by the law package.
Exactness
The measured witness or value is the intended in-scope law reading.
Lawfulness bridge
The assumptions connecting selected lawfulness to selected obstruction-circuit absence.
