The SFT program is not a backlog of open questions. It is a ladder: define the bounded objects, compute with them, calibrate them, and close the loop through governance and observed outcomes.

Research ladder

  1. Formal core.State bounded field objects, support relations, step relations, cones, envelope projections, and safety claims.
  2. SFT workbench.Build report shapes that make assumptions, supports, policies, horizons, and unknown remainders visible.
  3. Calibration.Compare forecast items with observed PRs, reviews, CI outcomes, incidents, and post-change architecture movement.
  4. Governance experiments.Study how review rules, AI proposal policies, lifecycle choices, and feedback loops alter supported paths.
  5. Promotion discipline.Promote a claim only when its proof, tooling, or empirical evidence matches the level being asserted.

Benchmarks

SFT needs benchmarks that preserve claim boundaries. An AI governance benchmark should track proposal support, rejected shortcuts, review mediation, and post-merge outcomes. A lifecycle benchmark should compare migration, repair, contraction, deletion, and end-of-life decisions as field interventions rather than as isolated code edits.

Calibration is the bridge from useful tooling to empirical claim. Until forecast items are joined with observed outcomes, the system remains a structured review aid.

Closed-loop tooling

The long-term tool shape is closed-loop: artifact descriptors and field estimates produce support estimates and consequence envelopes; review, CI, runtime feedback, incidents, and accepted or rejected proposals update the field estimate; future governance is then evaluated against the updated field.

This loop is a research program because each step has a different maturity level. Some parts are formal targets, some are report schemas, some are empirical protocols, and some are organizational interventions to be evaluated in practice.
