Support Safety and Cone Narrowing

Support safety asks whether a selected support relation and step semantics keep reachable paths inside a selected safe region. Cone narrowing studies how added constraints reduce reachable futures under the chosen boundary.

Question

When does a policy actually narrow the future paths that matter?

Support safety

Support safety is relative to selected predicates. It may ask whether every supported step preserves a selected invariant, whether a script remains inside a safe region, or whether one support relation simulates another over the measured axes.

This is where SFT becomes precise about the word "safe." A supported operation can be natural without being safe. A selected policy can reject some bad paths without controlling all future risk. Support safety therefore names a bounded claim: under this support relation, this step semantics, and this selected safe region, the modeled paths remain inside the region or simulate a safer system.

The value is not that SFT suddenly proves global software safety. The value is that it can state exactly which safety claim is being made and which part of the cone it applies to.

This makes support safety useful in review. Instead of saying "this policy makes the system safe," a review can ask whether this policy removes a selected unsupported operation, whether the remaining steps simulate a safer transition system, or whether the cone merely looks narrower because unmeasured paths were left outside the slice.

Definition 2.15. Support safety is a boundary-relative claim that supported steps preserve a selected safe region or simulate a selected safer transition system.

SFT status formal target Source: SFT sections 15 and 16; global safety is not claimed.

Cone narrowing

If a policy removes unsafe support, adds missing witnesses, or makes bad shortcuts expensive, a selected cone projection may narrow. The word "selected" matters. A cone can narrow over measured axes while unmeasured axes remain unknown, and a safe projected cone need not imply global system safety.

Narrowing is most interesting when it changes naturalness, not only when it adds a gate. A review rule that merely rejects a shortcut after it is proposed may leave the shortcut cheap. A better intervention makes the lawful path visible, testable, and easier to select, while making the shortcut costly, observable, or unsupported. The cone narrows because the field has changed.

This is the computational reading of good governance. It is not only a filter at the end of a proposal stream. It is a transformation of support. If the intervention works, the next reachable futures have a different shape.

The subtle point is that narrowing can be good, bad, or merely apparent. It is good when it removes unsupported or unsafe paths while preserving useful architecture futures. It is bad when it blocks necessary repair paths. It is apparent when the model has only narrowed what it can see. SFT keeps the selected safe region and observation boundary attached to the claim so this difference remains visible.

Computation habit. Treat a narrowed cone as a selected theorem-shaped claim, not as a universal statement that the system is now safe.

Boundary

Non-conclusion 2.16. Cone narrowing over selected axes is not global risk reduction, not incident prevention, and not proof that unmeasured paths are safe.
