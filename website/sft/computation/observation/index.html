Tooling layer

ArchSig scans and reports selected structural and artifact evidence. In AAT terms, it can expose signature axes, witnesses, measured and unmeasured components, and theorem precondition status. In SFT terms, those observations become part of a field estimate or a forecast report input.

Observation is the first place where the large field becomes accountable. A field may contain review memory, runtime history, ownership, artifacts, and local patterns, but SFT cannot compute from everything at once. It needs selected observations: measured axes, extracted witnesses, artifact refs, and explicit unknowns.

Observation is not interpretation. A warning does not prove an incident cause. A measured zero is not the same as an unmeasured axis. A report can support review by making boundaries visible, but it does not replace the theorem or the calibrated empirical study.

The strength of an observation layer is not that it sees everything. It is that it says what it saw and what it did not see. A tooling report that preserves unknown remainder is more useful to SFT than a polished score that hides the measurement boundary.

This changes the role of tooling. The tool is not there to pronounce the architecture good or bad. It is there to make selected coordinates available to the field computation. A signature axis can become a path coordinate. An obstruction candidate can become an envelope item. A missing theorem premise can become a governance question instead of being silently ignored.

A good observation layer also slows down false certainty. If a report can say "this axis was not measured," then later pages can keep that absence alive inside the ForecastCone and ConsequenceEnvelope. That is more valuable than a cleaner report that forgets its own blindness.

Observable surface

  • signature axes Selected multi-axis diagnostic values, not a single architecture score.
  • obstruction candidates Evidence that a local law or intended invariant may be blocked.
  • theorem boundary Which formal preconditions are observed, missing, or outside the tool boundary.
  • unknown remainder Private, unavailable, framework-specific, dynamic, or unmodeled evidence that must not be silently treated as absent.

Definition 2.5. An observation layer maps real artifacts into selected AAT observables and SFT field-estimate evidence while preserving measurement boundary and non-conclusions.

This is why ArchSig is neither AAT nor SFT. It is a bridge from real artifacts to bounded evidence. On the AAT side, it helps expose local algebraic coordinates. On the SFT side, it helps construct field estimates and forecast inputs. It does not prove the local theorem, and it does not certify the future.

The same observation can therefore play different roles. A signature axis can be an AAT measurement coordinate, an SFT path coordinate, a review concern, and a calibration feature. The role changes with the surrounding claim boundary.

Computation habit. Treat every observation as selected evidence, not as the whole system finally revealed.

Boundary

Non-conclusion 2.6. ArchSig output is not a Lean theorem, not a complete architecture model, and not a calibrated SFT forecast. It is bounded evidence for later reasoning.
