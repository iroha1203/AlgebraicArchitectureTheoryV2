Computable Slice

A development field is too large to compute directly. SFT begins computation by declaring what is inside the model, what evidence supports it, and what remains outside as unknown remainder.

From field to estimate

The full DevelopmentField includes code, artifacts, people, review practice, operations, incidents, governance, and private or missing context. A computable slice does not erase that scale. It records a boundary and constructs a partial SoftwareFieldEstimate from the evidence available inside it.

The first computational act is therefore not calculation. It is selection. We choose which artifacts count as evidence, which repository surfaces are visible, which review traces can be read, which runtime signals are available, and which parts of the organization must remain outside the model. Without this cut, "compute software evolution" is only a slogan.

The cut is not a retreat from the ambition of SFT. It is the move that makes the ambition usable. A bounded slice lets us ask what follows from the evidence we have, while keeping the unobserved remainder explicit instead of smuggling it into a false sense of completeness.

A good slice has tension. If it is too small, it becomes a static architecture metric and loses field dynamics. If it is too large, it becomes an essay rather than a computation. The modeling boundary is where SFT chooses enough structure to calculate without pretending to have captured the whole organization.

This is why SFT insists on unknown remainder. Missing evidence is not an inconvenience to hide. It is a first-class part of the estimate. A private incident, an unavailable review thread, a dynamic dependency, or an unmeasured runtime axis may be exactly the thing that separates a useful forecast from a confident fiction.

Definition 2.1. A computable slice is a modeling boundary together with the evidence categories used to assemble a partial SoftwareFieldEstimate.

SFT status conceptual definition Source: SFT section 3 and the Software Field Reconstruction Protocol.

Architecture projection

The slice must also decide how the field is projected onto architecture. That projection is where AAT can enter: components, dependencies, operations, invariant families, signatures, and obstruction witnesses can be read as selected coordinates of the field.

The projection is not the whole field. It is the part of the field where architecture laws and tool observations can be stated clearly. A field estimate may preserve review history, lifecycle pressure, and missing evidence even when the architecture projection only exposes selected structural axes.

This is where the theory becomes interesting rather than merely cautious. The architecture projection gives the field a local algebraic face. It lets SFT talk about operations, invariants, signatures, and witnesses without pretending that the social and operational field has disappeared. The projection is a coordinate system, not the territory.

The projection also creates the bridge to later pages. Operation support can refer to operations over the field. StepRelation can update the projected architecture and the surrounding evidence. ForecastCone can then range over field paths whose architecture shadows are visible through selected axes.

Once this projection exists, the field can be read at two levels at once. Locally, AAT can ask whether an operation preserves a selected invariant. Globally, SFT can ask whether the field keeps making that operation reachable, selected, or neglected.

Field habit. Treat every computed result as relative to its slice: boundary, evidence, axes, horizon, and missing remainder.

Boundary

Non-conclusion 2.2. A SoftwareFieldEstimate is not complete reconstruction of an organization, and an architecture projection is not ground truth about every force acting on future work.
