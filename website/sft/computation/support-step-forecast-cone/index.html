Artifact action

Part 2 begins before code changes. A PRD, spec, issue, design note, or AI proposal acts on the field by opening candidate update paths. It changes what developers consider natural, reviewable, cheap, forbidden, or urgent. SFT calls this an artifact-mediated change.

Vocabulary

ArtifactAction

A set-valued action from a current field to candidate update descriptors under an explicit interpretation boundary.

Source
PRD, issue, spec, review request, incident report, or AI-generated proposal.
Effect
Changes candidate support, policy, constraints, observation, and forecast boundary.

This is why SFT is not a PRD-to-PR predictor. The artifact does not contain a single future diff. It creates a family of possible field updates, each with its own support and forecast boundary.

Object What it carries
RawArtifactAction source artifact, target field components, interpretation boundary, candidate updates, action boundary, composition boundary, observable boundary, and non-conclusions.
ForceDescriptor action class, target architecture regions, candidate operation support, affected policy, constraints, governance, observation, and uncertainty boundary.
ForecastedEffect forecast boundary, expected comparable axes, expected delta ranges, obstruction witness families, and unknown remainder.
ObservedEffect observed transition, before/after signatures, comparable measured delta, unexpected witnesses, review, CI, and runtime outcome.

No circular forecast. The raw action does not contain the forecast. The action supplies candidate pressure; the forecast is computed from that pressure under a field model.

Computational core

The machinery is best read as a sequence of translations. Each stage turns a living development situation into a more precise object while keeping enough context to remain useful for engineering judgment.

Lean-facing SFT isolates the theorem-bearing fragment: fields, supported operations, step relations, bounded paths, cone projection, envelope projection, support safety, and update records. The rest of the organization remains in the field model and observation boundary.

Operation support

Operation support is the SFT object that captures what the field makes possible, natural, low-cost, reviewable, or selected. It is not yet safety. It is the shape of what can plausibly happen next under the selected field model.

Vocabulary

OperationSupport

The operation family available at a field state after selected constraints, governance, evidence, and policy are taken into account.

Question
Which changes are available, natural, low-cost, reviewable, or selected?
Boundary
Support is not safety; it is the field-relative space of plausible next operations.

Policy and selection

Support says what is available. Policy says what is easy, preferred, cheaper, or likely to be selected inside that support. This distinction is the computational version of field force: two operations can both be possible while one is much more natural for the current organization, tooling, review culture, and codebase memory.

Vocabulary

OperationPolicy

A preorder, cost model, selection relation, or distribution over supported operations in a selected field.

Minimal core
Preorder, cost, or selected-operation predicate.
Extended semantics
Probability distribution only when a stochastic model is explicitly chosen.

Step relation

A StepRelation says when a supported operation can move one selected field estimate to another. Once steps exist, software evolution is no longer only a sequence of commits; it is a path object with witnesses, support, and observation.

Vocabulary

StepRelation

The relation that says a supported operation can move one selected field estimate to another.

Input
Field F, operation op, candidate next field F'.
Output
A witnessed step that can be composed into bounded field paths.

ForecastCone

Vocabulary

ForecastCone

The bounded set of reachable field paths generated from a starting field, a support relation, step semantics, and a finite horizon.

Shape
Path set, not point prediction.
Use
Lets support inclusion, step simulation, and cone narrowing become theorem-shaped questions.

The cone is the central mathematical image of SFT. It is not a point prediction. It is the bounded shape of reachable futures under stated support, step semantics, and horizon.

Cone narrowing is the first theorem-shaped promise of this machinery. If a new constraint preserves the intended feature direction while removing a selected witness family, and if the narrower support simulates into the wider support, then the narrower cone projects into the wider cone over the same horizon.

Reachability regions

Once the cone is explicit, SFT can ask several different reachability questions instead of compressing them into one risk score. May-reach, must-reach, stable-region, and reachable preimage are different computational objects.

Vocabulary

StableRegion

A region of field states closed under selected accepted steps.

May reach
At least one supported path reaches the target region within horizon.
Must reach
Every selected supported path reaches the target region within horizon.

This is the careful version of attractor language. If a team wants to say a field has an attractor, SFT asks which formal structure supports that word: stability, recurrence, policy preference, probability, or only a visible default path.

Support safety

Definition 2-2. Support safety asks whether selected supported steps preserve a selected safe region or simulate a safer transition system over a bounded horizon.

Computation habit. First compute supported paths. Then ask which selected constraints narrow or preserve the paths you care about.

Proposition 2-2. Pointwise support inclusion plus step simulation gives a projection from the narrower ForecastCone into the wider ForecastCone over the same bounded horizon.

Proof sketch. Induct on the horizon. At horizon zero, the starting fields are related by assumption. At the successor horizon, take a supported step in the narrower system. Step simulation supplies a corresponding step in the wider system and preserves the field relation. Apply the induction hypothesis to the remaining suffix.

Governance intervention

Review, CI, type checking, architecture rules, runtime guards, and AI policy are not only gates at the end of development. In SFT they are transformations of support, policy, observation, and feedback. A governance system changes which future paths are easy, visible, expensive, escalated, or remembered.

Intervention Computational effect
Restrictive Removes unsafe or unsupported operation families.
Redirective Raises shortcut cost and lowers lawful path cost.
Instrumenting Adds observation axes, tests, traces, or runtime checks.
Escalating Turns proposal pressure into review, design, or incident records.
Learning Writes observed outcomes back into field memory.
