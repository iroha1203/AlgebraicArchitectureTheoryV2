Accounting

Proposal accounting records the difference between pressure and transition. Human developers, AI agents, issue templates, PRDs, and local code patterns can all generate proposal pressure. Only some proposals become accepted field steps. Others are rejected, reworked, deferred, or converted into new artifacts.

This distinction matters because proposal pressure is already field evidence. A bad proposal may show that a shortcut is cheap. A repeated AI suggestion may show that local examples are pulling the model toward the same basin. A rejected human proposal may reveal that the policy is doing useful work, or that the field has failed to make the lawful path visible.

If SFT only records merged changes, it misses the pressure that shaped the future. Proposal accounting keeps the rejected, delayed, and reworked paths visible so they can update the field estimate.

This is especially important for AI-mediated work. A model may generate ten local shortcuts before one is accepted. If the nine rejected shortcuts disappear from the record, the field loses evidence about what it is making easy for the agent to generate. Proposal accounting preserves that pressure as data.

The same is true for human teams. A rejected migration proposal, an abandoned refactor, or a deferred cleanup can show that a lawful path exists but is too expensive under the current field. If those events are not counted, SFT sees only the changes that survived selection, not the selection pressure itself.

Definition 2.17. Proposal accounting is the field-relative bookkeeping that separates generated proposal pressure from accepted field transitions and review feedback.

Review mediation

Review is not merely a quality gate. It changes what the field remembers. A rejected shortcut can make an invariant visible. A requested test can become future support. A missed review can make a path cheaper next time. Accepted change and future support preservation must therefore be evaluated separately.

The most important review outcome may be neither approval nor rejection. It may be the new artifact left behind: a test, an issue split, a policy note, a named boundary, or a warning that future agents can read. In SFT, review mediates the field by changing what later proposals will see as natural.

This lets review become part of computation rather than an external human judgment box. A review event can be modeled as a field update, a support adjustment, a policy refinement, or a calibration reference for a prior forecast item.

That is why proposal accounting sits after support safety. Safety and narrowing ask what a policy can constrain. Proposal accounting asks what the field actually generated and how governance responded. The difference between those two views is where field learning happens.

Computation habit. Count proposal pressure separately from accepted transition. A field can generate dangerous futures even when review rejects the first instance.

Boundary

Non-conclusion 2.18. Acceptance of a proposal does not prove support safety, and rejection of a proposal does not prove the field was safe. Review outcomes are evidence for later field updates and calibration.
