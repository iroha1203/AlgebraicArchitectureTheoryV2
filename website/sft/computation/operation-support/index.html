Question

Why do some architecture-preserving changes remain invisible while shortcuts become the easy path?

Naturalness is computable evidence

In ordinary engineering language, we often ask whether a change is correct. SFT asks a prior question: why did that change appear as the obvious move? The obvious move is not always the lawful move. It is the move the field made cheap enough, visible enough, and defensible enough to propose.

This is the intellectual center of Part 2. Software evolution is not driven only by the set of valid architecture operations. It is also driven by the set of operations that are easy to find, easy to justify, easy for AI to generate, easy for reviewers to understand, easy for CI to miss, or easy to merge without crossing ownership boundaries.

SFT treats that difference as a computational object. The field does not merely contain possible operations. It supports some operations more strongly than others. The support can be estimated, compared, narrowed, and updated after review or runtime feedback.

Computation habit. Before asking whether an operation is right, ask why the field made it available.

Support

OperationSupport names the operation families a field makes available under selected evidence. Support is not mere permission. It includes visibility, cost, reviewability, ownership, tooling help, policy pressure, local examples, and missing evidence that makes an operation hard to distinguish from a shortcut.

A field may support a good operation by giving it names, tests, ownership, examples, and review language. The same field may support a bad operation by leaving a shortcut nearby, by hiding the invariant, or by making the lawful path require exceptional coordination. Both are support facts. SFT does not assume that supported means desirable.

Definition 2.7. Operation support is the selected field-relative evidence that an operation family is available, legible, low-cost, or likely to be chosen under a policy boundary.

Policy and cost

OperationPolicy selects among supported operations. A policy may come from review rules, CI requirements, ownership approval, incident response, AI-agent constraints, or lifecycle pressure. SFT keeps policy separate from support because a field can make an operation visible while governance rejects it, or make a weak operation easy while policy fails to detect it.

Cost is not just runtime or implementation effort. It includes the cost of finding the right owner, explaining the invariant, crossing a team boundary, writing the missing test, touching a fragile module, or asking an AI agent to reason beyond local pattern completion. These costs shape which future becomes reachable in practice.

This is not project-management scoring. The point is to model how the field changes the support of future architecture paths. A low-cost shortcut can be more dangerous than an obviously bad proposal, because it arrives wearing the field's own vocabulary of naturalness.

Selection pressure

Once support and policy are separated, review outcomes become more informative. A rejected proposal can show that a path was supported but not policy-admissible. A merged proposal can show that a path was selected, but not that it preserved future support. A repeated family of AI proposals can show that the field is teaching agents to see one operation family as the natural basin.

This is where SFT begins to become calculable. Given a selected field estimate, we can ask which operations have support, which policies select or reject them, and what paths become reachable when those choices are repeated over a bounded horizon.

Computation habit. A shortcut is not only a bad operation. It is evidence that the field made the wrong future easy.

Boundary

Non-conclusion 2.8. Operation support is not a claim that an operation is correct, safe, or inevitable. It is evidence about availability and selection under a bounded field model.
