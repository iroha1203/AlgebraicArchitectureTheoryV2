How can a field too large to know become a bounded object of computation?

Slice

SFT does not compute by pretending to know the whole organization. It computes by choosing a modeling boundary: selected artifacts, selected codebase region, selected architecture universe, selected observation axes, selected governance records, selected horizon, and explicit unknown remainder.

That boundary is not a compromise with the theory. It is the move that makes the theory mathematical. Without a selected boundary, "the field" is too large to inspect. With a selected boundary, the field becomes a state estimate whose evidence, missing evidence, private evidence, and unknown remainder can travel through later computations.

Vocabulary

Modeling boundary

The selected slice of a development field that a computation is allowed to use.

Includes
Artifacts, code region, architecture universe, axes, governance records, horizon, and unknown remainder.
Purpose
Makes the field computable without pretending to reconstruct the whole organization.

AAT projection

A SoftwareField is not an AAT ArchitectureObject. The AAT object appears as an architecture projection of the field. AAT supplies the local algebra: operations, invariants, witnesses, signatures, and theorem boundaries that SFT can use as local premises.

This is the main mathematical interface. SFT inherits AAT's local discipline without asking AAT to become an evolution theory. An AAT operation can become a local transition premise. An invariant family can become a selected safe-region predicate. An obstruction witness can become a forecast axis or repair target. A signature can become a coordinate on a field trajectory.

Vocabulary

Architecture projection

The AAT-readable architecture object extracted from a selected field estimate.

SFT then introduces its own evolution-level vocabulary on top of that projection: force, field state, trajectory, operation policy, ForecastCone, ConsequenceEnvelope, forecast boundary, proposal accounting, feedback update, stable regions, AI operation pressure, and lifecycle dynamics. These are not AAT concepts. They are the extra field machinery that lets local architecture algebra participate in software evolution.

Interface rule. AAT theorem boundaries become SFT premises and forecast boundaries. They do not become empirical prediction theorems by crossing the interface.

Claim translation

The interface is not only a dependency arrow. It is a translation discipline. SFT reads AAT statements as local premises inside a field computation. The claim level is preserved when it crosses the boundary: a local theorem becomes a local law premise, a tooling report becomes a field estimate, and an empirical hypothesis becomes a calibrated forecast only after data enters.

AAT side SFT reading Not this

obstruction witness repair target or forecast boundary axis physical resistance or future certainty

This is why SFT can be ambitious without smuggling in stronger claims. AAT lawfulness can constrain a local transition. It cannot, by itself, certify trajectory-level safety. A measured signature coordinate can support comparison. It cannot, by itself, become empirical prediction.

ArchSig bridge

ArchSig is the bridge between real artifacts and the projected architecture object. It is neither AAT nor SFT. It is the tooling layer that turns repositories, diffs, reports, reviews, and runtime evidence into bounded observations that both sides can read.

The bridge matters because the same artifact can play different roles. For AAT, it may expose component universes, signature axes, obstruction witnesses, and theorem-boundary status. For SFT, it may become a force descriptor, operation-support estimate, forecast-boundary item, trajectory observation, or feedback update input.

Layer Reads from artifact as
AAT signature axes, witnesses, local operations, theorem-boundary status
SFT field estimate, force descriptor, support estimate, forecast boundary, feedback input
Reviewer affected region, missing invariant, review question, CI or issue decomposition cue

ArchSig observation

ArchSig is the tooling layer that turns real artifacts into bounded observations: measured signature axes, obstruction witnesses, theorem-boundary notes, unavailable evidence, and unknown remainder. It is what lets the theory remain connected to repositories without claiming complete extraction.

The key rule is that absence and unavailability are different. A measured zero means a selected coordinate was measured as zero. It does not mean every unmeasured axis is safe. A private incident record, an unavailable runtime trace, or an unsupported extractor axis must remain visible as a boundary item.

Vocabulary

Observation boundary

The record of what was measured, unavailable, private, unsupported, or still unknown in the selected slice.

Measured
Comparable signature axes, witnesses, theorem-boundary notes.
Not measured
Unavailable and private evidence remain boundary items, not zeros.

Field estimate

Definition 2-1. A computable slice is a modeling boundary together with the evidence categories and architecture projection used to assemble a partial SoftwareFieldEstimate.

SFT status formal-facing definition Feeds the support, step, and cone objects in Part 2-2.

Proposition 2-1. A SoftwareFieldEstimate is sufficient for bounded SFT computation when it records selected evidence, selected architecture projection, observation axes, and unknown remainder.

Proof idea. The later objects only require the selected field state, support relation, policy or selection assumptions, step relation, observation boundary, and horizon. Evidence outside the estimate is not erased; it is carried as unavailable or unknown remainder. Therefore the computation can proceed without claiming complete field reconstruction.

Counterexample 2-1. If a repository scan omits private incident evidence but treats the omission as absence, it can make an unsafe coupon path look unsupported by any warning. The estimate must preserve unavailable evidence as a boundary item, not as zero.
