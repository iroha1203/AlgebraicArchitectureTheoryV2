How can a field too large to know become a bounded object of computation?

Slice

SFT does not compute by pretending to know the whole organization. It computes by choosing a modeling boundary: selected artifacts, selected codebase region, selected architecture universe, selected observation axes, selected governance records, selected horizon, and explicit unknown remainder.

AAT projection

A SoftwareField is not an AAT ArchitectureObject. The AAT object appears as an architecture projection of the field. AAT supplies the local algebra: operations, invariants, witnesses, signatures, and theorem boundaries that SFT can use as local premises.

ArchSig observation

ArchSig is the tooling layer that turns real artifacts into bounded observations: measured signature axes, obstruction witnesses, theorem-boundary notes, unavailable evidence, and unknown remainder. It is what lets the theory remain connected to repositories without claiming complete extraction.

Field estimate

Definition 2-1. A computable slice is a modeling boundary together with the evidence categories and architecture projection used to assemble a partial SoftwareFieldEstimate.

SFT status formal-facing definition Feeds the support, step, and cone objects in Part 2-2.

Proposition 2-1. A SoftwareFieldEstimate is sufficient for bounded SFT computation when it records selected evidence, selected architecture projection, observation axes, and unknown remainder.

Proof idea. The later objects only require the selected field state, support relation, policy or selection assumptions, step relation, observation boundary, and horizon. Evidence outside the estimate is not erased; it is carried as unavailable or unknown remainder. Therefore the computation can proceed without claiming complete field reconstruction.

Counterexample 2-1. If a repository scan omits private incident evidence but treats the omission as absence, it can make an unsafe coupon path look unsupported by any warning. The estimate must preserve unavailable evidence as a boundary item, not as zero.

Chapter summary

SFT becomes computable by cutting a slice from a larger development field. The slice is not a confession that the field metaphor failed; it is the mathematical move that makes field dynamics inspectable: selected evidence, selected axes, selected architecture projection, and explicit unknown remainder.

AAT and ArchSig protect the slice. AAT supplies the local algebra of architecture objects, operations, invariants, signatures, and witnesses. ArchSig supplies bounded observation. Lean-facing SFT then reasons about the selected SoftwareFieldEstimate without pretending to have extracted the whole organization.

Chapter glossary

modeling boundary
The selected artifacts, code region, architecture universe, axes, governance records, horizon, and unknown remainder.
architecture projection
The AAT-readable object extracted from a SoftwareField for local algebra and theorem premises.
observation boundary
The measured, unavailable, private, out-of-scope, and unknown evidence status for later computation.
