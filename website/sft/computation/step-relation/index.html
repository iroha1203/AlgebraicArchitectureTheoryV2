Field transition

A StepRelation relates a field estimate, a supported operation, and a successor field estimate. It is where operation support becomes transition semantics. The relation may update code, observations, policy state, review memory, artifact state, or unknown remainder.

This is the moment where SFT stops being only a vocabulary for pressure and starts becoming a dynamics. A supported operation is still only a candidate. A field step says what happens when that operation is modeled as changing the field: what architecture projection moves, what evidence is added, what policy state changes, and what uncertainty remains.

The successor field is not merely "the new code." It may include a rejected proposal, a new test, a review decision, an incident note, an updated ownership boundary, or a newly visible unknown. This is why field paths can represent software evolution more faithfully than code diffs alone.

This is also where lifecycle and governance begin to matter computationally. A deletion can be a field step. A migration rehearsal can be a field step. A review that blocks a proposal but records a missing invariant can be a field step. SFT can therefore count learning and constraint changes as part of evolution, not just merged source edits.

Definition 2.9. A field step is a bounded transition from one field estimate to another, witnessed by a selected supported operation and its modeled effects.

Bounded paths

A path keeps the trajectory visible. Two paths may end at similar signatures while passing through different risk regions. One path may preserve a selected invariant step by step; another may have safe endpoints but unsafe intermediate states. This is why Part 2 builds ForecastCone from bounded paths, not from endpoint labels alone.

The path view is essential because many architecture failures are trajectory failures. A migration may end cleanly while spending months in a state where rollback is impossible. A refactor may reach the intended dependency direction while losing runtime observability. A review policy may block one bad step while making a different shortcut cheaper next time.

Bounded paths let SFT ask what must be preserved at every step, not only what the final diagram should look like. They are the skeleton on which ForecastCone is built.

The bounded horizon is not arbitrary decoration. It is what makes path reasoning finite. With a horizon, SFT can ask what is reachable in one step, two steps, or within a review cycle. Different horizons answer different questions, and the chosen horizon must stay visible in the forecast.

Field habit. Ask which path class is supported, not only which destination is desired.

Boundary

Non-conclusion 2.10. A supported path is not necessarily a safe path. Safety requires separate selected regions, preservation assumptions, and observation boundaries.
