ForecastCone

A ForecastCone is the set of bounded future paths reachable from a field estimate under selected support, policy, and horizon. It is a reachability object, not a single prediction.

Question

What futures remain reachable if we fix the field model, support relation, policy, and horizon?

Why a cone?

Point prediction is the wrong object for software evolution. The next PR is selected by humans, tools, review, deadlines, failures, and organizational memory. SFT therefore does not ask the theory to guess one future. It asks a sharper question: under this selected field, what futures remain reachable?

The answer has a shape. From the current field, one family of operations may open several paths. Some paths preserve a boundary. Some make a shortcut cheaper. Some expose a missing invariant. Some become impossible once governance records a new rule. As the horizon grows, the reachable region widens. That widening region is the intuition behind the cone.

This is the dangerous beauty of SFT: it moves the object from "what will happen?" to "what has this field made possible?" The latter can be bounded, inspected, compared, narrowed, and calibrated against later evidence.

Cone semantics

The cone begins at a selected field estimate. At each step, it follows operations supported by the field and accepted by the selected policy. The horizon bounds how far the computation is allowed to unfold. Every part of the object is relative: relative to the field estimate, relative to the support family, relative to the policy, and relative to the observation boundary.

This definition is intentionally modest. It does not promise probability, agency, or destiny. It gives SFT a finite object to compute with: a bounded family of field paths whose witnesses must be recorded, whose unknown remainder must be preserved, and whose interpretation must stay inside the selected boundary.

Definition 2.11. A ForecastCone is a bounded set of reachable field paths relative to a start field, support family, policy assumptions, and horizon.

After artifact action

A PRD, spec, issue, incident report, or AI proposal may change support before any code is changed. SFT can therefore compare cone families before and after an artifact action. Which future path families become newly supported? Which become harder? Which shortcuts become low-cost? Which observations remain missing?

This comparison is useful precisely because it is not a point prediction. It tells reviewers what the artifact makes reachable under the selected model. A vague artifact may widen the cone by allowing many plausible operations. A precise artifact may narrow it by naming the invariant and boundary. An AI proposal may open a large family of local-looking paths that governance must now account for.

Computation habit. Do not ask whether the artifact predicts the PR. Ask how it changes the reachable shape of future paths.

What the shape tells us

The shape of a cone can reveal the field. A narrow cone may mean the field has strong invariants and clear policy, or it may mean the model has ignored too much. A wide cone may mean rich design freedom, or it may mean ambiguity. A cone that repeatedly bends toward the same local shortcut suggests an attractor. A cone that only reaches a clean endpoint through unsafe intermediate paths exposes trajectory risk.

This is why the cone is a research object rather than a UI widget. It lets SFT compare fields, artifacts, policies, and interventions by their effect on reachable futures. Part 2 then needs the next object: a way to project this large theoretical cone into something a reviewer can read. That projection is the ConsequenceEnvelope.

Boundary

Non-conclusion 2.12. A ForecastCone does not claim probability weighting, forecast correctness, causal proof, global safety, or that a team will actually choose one of its paths.
