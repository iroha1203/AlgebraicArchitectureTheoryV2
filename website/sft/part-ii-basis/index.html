Boundary note

AAT theorem status is not converted into empirical prediction status. SFT uses AAT results as local premises inside bounded field models.

Reader model

Part II is the interface chapter. It gives a website-local way to check what SFT is allowed to inherit from AAT, what SFT adds on its own side, and which readings are explicitly blocked.

SFT says / SFT does not say
SFT says SFT does not say
AAT supplies local algebra, theorem boundaries, and selected non-conclusions. AAT theorem status automatically becomes empirical forecast status.
AAT claims may be used as local premises inside bounded SFT reachability or safety schemas. AAT lawfulness alone proves future trajectory safety for a software field.
ArchSig supplies measured axes, missing axes, evidence boundaries, and tooling estimates. ArchSig output is a ground-truth architecture object or a Lean theorem.
SFT introduces field state, trajectories, forecast boundaries, and feedback governance. SFT redefines AAT concepts or makes AAT depend on software-field dynamics.

Dependency direction

SFT sits on AAT, but AAT does not depend on SFT. AAT turns software architecture into local algebra. SFT uses that algebra as architecture projection, local transition law, observable coordinate, and admissibility boundary.

  • Architecture projection SFT reads ArchitectureObject as the architecture object projected from a SoftwareField.
  • Local transition premise ArchitectureOperation becomes a local premise for accepted or proposed field transitions.
  • Forecast boundary AAT theorem boundaries and non-conclusions become SFT forecast boundaries.

The dependency arrow is one-way: AAT remains an independent local algebra of architecture, while SFT uses selected AAT objects as premises, coordinates, and boundaries for bounded software-evolution models.

Borrowed from AAT

SFT borrows AAT terms by role. The table is not an empirical promotion rule; it records which AAT surface may appear inside an SFT model and what job it performs there.

AAT terms used by SFT
SFT-side role AAT surface Use inside SFT
Architecture projection ArchitectureObject The projected architecture object obtained from a selected SoftwareField slice.
Local transition law ArchitectureOperation A local admissibility premise for accepted or proposed field transitions.
Protected constraint InvariantFamily The selected constraint family a transition is expected to preserve.
Defect / repair target ObstructionWitness A selected unsafe, costly, or blocked trajectory coordinate, not a complete latent-cause inventory.
Partial observation coordinate ArchitectureSignature An axis-wise coordinate system for observing selected architecture states and deltas.
Admissibility boundary Theorem boundary / non-conclusions Conditions that SFT forecasts and governance reports must preserve as claim boundaries.
Local path skeleton ArchitecturePath A local skeleton for transition reasoning, not a complete history of software evolution.

SFT-side concepts

These concepts are introduced on the SFT side of the interface. They may use AAT projections and ArchSig observations, but they are not AAT definitions.

Concepts introduced by SFT
SFT concept Role Boundary
field state / SoftwareField The selected computable state of a development field. It is not itself an AAT ArchitectureObject; AAT sees the architecture projection.
trajectory A modeled sequence or set of possible field transitions. It is not proven safe by local AAT lawfulness alone.
ForecastCone A bounded set of possible future states under stated support. Cone narrowing is not global risk reduction without calibration and stated axes.
ConsequenceEnvelope A boundary-aware report of expected effects, missing axes, and non-conclusions. It is a forecast artifact, not a theorem unless separately formalized as a schema.
field update / feedback governance Rules for updating estimates and review policy from observations. Operational usefulness does not promote correlation to a formal or causal claim.

Translation rules

Interface terms are mappings, not redefinitions. SFT may use AAT vocabulary as local premises and observation axes, but SFT trajectory, forecast, policy, and feedback concepts remain SFT concepts.

One-way translation rules
AAT side Allowed SFT reading Claim boundary
ArchitectureObject Architecture projection of a field state. Does not describe the whole development field.
ArchitectureOperation Local law premise for an accepted or proposed transition. Does not establish global policy safety.
InvariantFamily Constraint family the field attempts to preserve. Applies only to selected invariants and stated assumptions.
ObstructionWitness Selected repair target or forecast boundary axis. Absence of the selected witness is not absence of latent action.
ArchitectureSignature Partial coordinate system for trajectory observation. Measured zero is not safety for unmeasured axes.
Theorem boundary / non-conclusions Forecast and governance claim boundary. Cannot be dropped when translating into tooling or review reports.
  • ArchitectureObject Read in SFT as the architecture projection of a field state.
  • ArchitectureOperation Read as a local law premise for an accepted or proposed transition.
  • InvariantFamily Read as a constraint family the field attempts to preserve.
  • ObstructionWitness Read as a selected unsafe, costly, or blocked trajectory coordinate.
  • ArchitectureSignature Read as a partial coordinate system for observing trajectories.
  • Theorem boundary / non-conclusions Read as the claim boundary that SFT forecasts and governance reports must not cross.

Forbidden readings

The interface exists to prevent accidental claim promotion. Local architecture facts do not become global software-history facts merely because they are useful premises in an SFT model.

Claim-preserving readings and blocked promotions
AAT / tooling fact Allowed SFT reading Forbidden reading
selected witness absence A selected local defect is not observed under the current measurement universe. Future trajectory safety, or absence of all latent action.
signature axis zero / measured zero A selected measured coordinate is zero. Unmeasured axes are also safe or zero.
operation preserves invariant One accepted transition is locally admissible under stated assumptions. The policy, organization, or future trajectory is globally safe.
theorem boundary A premise boundary for SFT reachability or safety schema. An empirical prediction theorem.
observed signature delta A comparable axis-wise trajectory observation. A unique causal identification of the artifact action.
ArchSig extraction A tooling estimate with measured axes and non-conclusions. A ground-truth architecture object or a Lean proof.
  • AAT lawfulness Do not read it as future trajectory safety.
  • AAT measured zero Do not read it as safety for unmeasured axes.
  • AAT operation preservation Do not read it as global policy safety.
  • SFT ForecastCone narrowing Do not read it as global risk reduction.
  • ArchSig extraction Do not read it as a ground-truth architecture object.
  • SFT forecast Do not read it as a Lean theorem.

ArchSig bridge

ArchSig is the observation layer between real artifacts, AAT observables, and SFT field estimates. It reports measured axes, unmeasured axes, out-of-scope axes, evidence boundaries, and measurement non-conclusions.

ArchSig bridge anchors
Layer Anchor Non-conclusion
Formal anchor AAT observables, theorem boundary status, selected invariant and witness families. Tool output does not strengthen an AAT theorem.
Computational anchor Measured signature axes, comparable deltas, missing invariants, expected axis delta ranges. Unmeasured does not mean zero, and comparable does not mean causal.
SFT estimate anchor Action class candidates, target regions, forecast boundary, unknown unmodeled remainder. A forecast artifact is not a Lean theorem and not a point prediction.

Signature deltas are defined only where both sides are measured and axis-wise comparison is available. Unmeasured does not mean zero, and tool output is not a theorem.

Claim levels

SFT claims must state their observation, model, and calibration basis. The same sentence can have different status depending on whether it is conceptual, trace-grounded, set-valued, probabilistic, calibrated, or operationally deployed.

  • Level 0 Conceptual or diagnostic interpretation.
  • Level 1 Trace-grounded field diagnosis.
  • Level 2 Set-valued formal theorem schema.
  • Level 3 and above Transition-kernel models, calibrated forecasts, or deployed closed-loop governance systems.

The levels keep three claims apart: a formal AAT theorem, a set-valued SFT schema over an explicit support relation, and an empirical forecast validated against traces or deployed outcomes.

Claim level transfer
AAT-side claim level SFT-side reading
AAT theorem level Local premise for an SFT theorem schema, such as reachability or safety under stated support.
AAT tooling claim SFT field estimate with evidence boundary and non-conclusions preserved.
AAT empirical hypothesis SFT calibrated forecast candidate, not a proved theorem.
AAT non-conclusions SFT forecast boundary items that must stay visible in reports and governance decisions.

A good specification may narrow a cone under support inclusion and step simulation. Without calibration, that is not an empirical prediction theorem. The SFT status page records the stronger evidence required for each promotion.

Basis boundary and non-conclusions

Part II is the bridge layer, so its main job is preventing category errors. ArchSig evidence may inform a field estimate, but it is not a Lean proof. AAT theorem status may supply a local premise, but it is not automatically software evolution forecast status.

  • Formal claim Lives in AAT or in a set-valued SFT schema with stated support and step semantics.
  • Tooling estimate Records measured axes, missing axes, evidence boundaries, and non-conclusions.
  • Empirical forecast Requires trace or dataset calibration; it is not obtained by renaming a theorem.
