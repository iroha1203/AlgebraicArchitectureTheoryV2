Introduction

Lehman's laws taught software engineering to stop imagining useful software as a finished artifact. Software connected to the real world must keep changing. Its environment changes, its users change, its organization changes, and the act of changing it produces new complexity that must itself be managed.

SFT stands on that line of thought and moves the question into the AI era. If software evolution is the life of the system itself, then architecture theory cannot look only at settled code. It must also look at the conditions that make the next change likely, cheap, visible, reviewable, or almost unthinkable.

This matters because AI coding agents make proposal generation cheap. When a team can produce many candidate PRs quickly, the central question moves upstream: what kind of future is the current development system teaching those proposals to pursue?

Chapters

Part 1 develops the field in three steps: codebase memory and artifact force, recurrent organization pressure, and governance feedback.

  • 1-1. Codebase Memory and Artifact Force Why codebases remember past work and why PRDs, issues, specs, reviews, and incidents act on future operation support.
  • 1-2. Agents, Organization, and Recurrent Paths How developers, AI agents, ownership, communication paths, and Conway-style pressure create recurrent development paths.
  • 1-3. Governance Feedback and Architecture Futures How review, CI, type checking, incidents, and postmortems update field memory and shape architecture futures.
