Positioning and Non-Conclusions

SFT is a theory of field-shaped software evolution. It borrows evidence from metrics, process, operations, formal methods, and AI evaluation, but it is not identical to any of them.

Not the same as

  • Static metric.SFT uses measurements, but its object is field-shaped reachable change, not one score.
  • Process model.Workflow matters because it changes support and observation; SFT is not a lifecycle checklist.
  • DevOps maturity model.CI and runtime feedback are field components, not maturity badges that imply architectural safety.
  • Socio-technical metaphor.Field language is a computational handle, not a claim that physical law governs development.
  • AI benchmark.AI proposals are one pressure source; SFT evaluates their support, review mediation, and consequences.
  • Formal verification.SFT can have formal fragments, but empirical reconstruction and forecast quality are not Lean theorems.

Adjacent roles

Static analysis, incident review, governance policy, and AI benchmark data can all become SFT evidence when their boundary is explicit. They help estimate field state, support, policy, or feedback. They do not replace the distinction between a mathematical claim, a tool artifact, and an empirical result.

This is why SFT can talk to many neighboring frameworks without collapsing into them. The translation question is always: which part of the field or cone does this evidence constrain?

Boundary rule

A statement should name its level: theorem-shaped claim, conceptual definition, tooling artifact, empirical hypothesis, calibrated empirical result, or non-conclusion. The level is part of the statement, not metadata added later.
