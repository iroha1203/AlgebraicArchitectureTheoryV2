Status and Claim Boundaries

SFT deliberately separates conceptual definitions, theorem-shaped statements, tooling artifacts, empirical hypotheses, calibrated results, and non-conclusions.

Claim levels

  • Conceptual definition.A stable vocabulary item in the SFT exposition, not yet a formal Lean object.
  • Theorem-shaped claim.A statement that can be made formal after objects, hypotheses, and non-conclusions are fixed.
  • Formal target.A candidate selected for Lean development or proof-obligation tracking.
  • Tooling artifact.A report, schema, or CLI output that supports review and calibration, not a proof.
  • Empirical hypothesis.A claim about observed development behavior that needs data and evaluation protocol.
  • Calibrated result.An empirical claim backed by matched forecast items and observed outcomes under a stated protocol.

Non-conclusions

A ForecastCone is not a point prediction, a ConsequenceEnvelope is not an incident-causality proof, and an ArchSig report is not a Lean certificate. SFT uses these objects because they make dangerous reasoning explicit, not because they remove uncertainty.

Promotion rules

  1. To formal claim.Provide definitions, hypotheses, proof statement, and a boundary saying what the theorem does not imply.
  2. To tooling claim.Provide schema, fixture, validation rule, and report interpretation rule.
  3. To empirical claim.Provide dataset boundary, matching protocol, measured outcome, uncertainty, and calibration limits.
